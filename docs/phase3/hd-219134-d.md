# HD 219134 d — Phase 3 Synthesis

HD 219134 d is an RV-only planet of the bright quiet K3 V dwarf
HD 219134, on a 46.71-day orbit at a = 0.23508 AU. It does **not**
transit, so the only directly measured physical quantity is the
minimum mass: Msini = 21.3 ± 1.27 M⊕ (Vogt et al. 2015, Keck/HIRES +
APT recommended; Motalebi 2015 gives a lower alternative Msini =
8.67 M⊕). With a minimum mass of ~21 M⊕ and no measured radius, d is
most plausibly a **warm mini-Neptune / sub-Neptune** with a volatile
envelope rather than a bare rock. The equilibrium temperature from the
project formula T_eq = 278.3 · L^0.25 / √a (L = 0.264) is 411 K at zero
albedo — warm, well above water's boiling point but far cooler than the
inner transiting pair.

**Scenario choice for NearStars: a warm mini-Neptune with a hydrogen-
helium-plus-volatile envelope, hazy zonal cloud bands rendered in warm
tones under K3 V orange illumination, and no measured radius (estimate-
only, low confidence).** At Msini ≈ 21 M⊕ and 411 K, d sits in the
sub-Neptune regime where a thick volatile atmosphere is expected. The
radius is unconstrained (RV-only), so all radius-dependent fields carry
low confidence; the cfg adopts a sub-Neptune mass-radius estimate. No
documented divergence — every value traces to the Vogt 2015 Phase 2
recommended mass, a shown derivation, or an interesting-first tie-break.

## Surface synthesis

HD 219134 d has no solid surface in the conventional sense — at Msini
≈ 21 M⊕ with no measured radius, it is most plausibly a warm
mini-Neptune / sub-Neptune with a thick volatile envelope over a
rocky-icy core. The "surface" relevant for visual rendering is the
cloud deck, conventionally referenced to the 1 bar pressure level.

The defining caveat is that **d does not transit**: its radius is
unmeasured, so every radius-dependent quantity (radius, surface
gravity, density, scale height) is an estimate flagged at low
confidence. The cfg adopts a sub-Neptune mass-radius scaling
(Lopez & Fortney 2014; Wolfgang 2016): for ~21 M⊕ with a modest
H/He + volatile envelope, R ≈ 2.5–3.5 R⊕, and the cfg picks 2.8 R⊕ as
the mid-range. This gives a surface gravity ~2.7 g⊕ and a bulk density
~5.3 g/cc, both consistent with a sub-Neptune (a rocky-icy core under a
gaseous envelope) rather than a bare rock — a 21 M⊕ pure-rock planet
would be far too dense and small. The radius could be revised
substantially by a future transit or direct-imaging detection.

**Color under K3 V illumination.** Under the 4858 K orange host SED,
the cloud deck and haze are perceived warmer than they would be under
Sol. The cfg renders a warm cream-tan cloud deck (`#e8d0aa`) with a
warm-tan haze limb (`#d8b890`). These are tie-break choices — no
spectrum exists for d — defaulting to the warm sub-Neptune banded
analog under orange-dwarf light.

## Atmosphere synthesis

The atmospheric inference for d is entirely theoretical (no transit, no
spectrum), but the mass and temperature place it confidently in the
sub-Neptune regime.

**Atmosphere present.** At Msini ≈ 21 M⊕ and T_eq ≈ 411 K, d sits well
above the rocky / sub-Neptune transition; the photoevaporation valley
(Owen & Wu 2017) leaves planets of this mass at 0.24 AU around a K
dwarf with their volatile envelopes intact. The cfg adopts a thick
H₂/He-dominated atmosphere with enhanced metallicity, a water-cloud
deck, and an overlying photochemical haze — the canonical warm
sub-Neptune composition. This is a tie-break composition (no measured
spectrum), defaulting to the standard sub-Neptune inventory.

**Sky appearance.** With a thick hazy atmosphere, the sky from the
(reference 1 bar) cloud deck is a warm-tan overcast, brightening toward
the substellar point. The host star HD 219134 subtends ~1.65° (about
3× the Sun's apparent size from Earth) and reads warm-orange through
the haze. Below the 1 bar level the atmosphere thickens into an opaque
warm envelope; above it, a thin photochemical haze layer scatters the
orange starlight.

## Rotation & spin synthesis

Unlike the close-in transiting pair, d is far enough out (0.235 AU,
46.71 d) that tidal locking is not assured. For a ~21 M⊕ volatile
planet at this separation around a 0.696 M☉ K dwarf, the tidal-
despinning timescale is comparable to or longer than the system age,
so the cfg adopts a **non-locked, slowly rotating** state rather than a
1:1 spin-orbit lock.

**KSP implementation note.** With no rotation measurement, the cfg
assigns a generic sub-Neptune rotation period (a slow ~1 day analog is
a reasonable placeholder; this is not a measured value and is flagged
low confidence). Obliquity is set to a small value as a tie-break.

**No strong seasons.** The curated eccentricity is 0.0 (circularized in
the Vogt 2015 fit), so insolation is steady around the orbit; there is
no eccentricity-driven seasonal modulation. Any weather banding is
driven by the internal heat flux and the steady 4.78 S⊕ insolation
rather than by seasons.

## Visual styling

Combining the atmosphere decisions for a warm RV-only sub-Neptune:

- **Global appearance (orbit view).** A warm cream-tan banded
  sub-Neptune disk, hazier and more featureless than a cold gas giant,
  with subtle zonal contrast under the orange host light.
- **Cloud bands.** Warm cream-tan zonal bands (`#e8d0aa`) with a water-
  cloud deck and an overlying photochemical haze; banded contrast is
  muted (a tie-break — no GCM, no spectrum).
- **Limb haze.** A warm-tan photochemical haze layer (`#d8b890`)
  scattering the orange starlight at the limb, giving a soft
  terminator rather than a sharp edge.
- **Star in sky.** HD 219134 subtends ~1.65° at d — about 3× the Sun's
  angular size from Earth, warm-orange through the haze, illuminating
  the cloud deck at ~4.8× Earth-noon brightness.
- **Sister planets in sky.** The inner transiting pair (b, c) and the
  contested f appear interior; the temperate g and cold giant h are
  exterior. All near-coplanar, appearing as bright points.
- **Low-confidence caveat (rendering note).** Because the radius is
  unmeasured, the rendered planet size is an estimate; the cfg should
  treat d's disk scale as provisional pending a transit or
  direct-imaging radius.

## Open items for follow-up

- **True mass and radius.** d's mass is Msini-only and the radius is a
  sub-Neptune scaling estimate at low confidence. A future transit
  detection, astrometric inclination (Gaia DR5+), or direct-imaging
  campaign would convert Msini to true mass and constrain the radius —
  all radius-dependent fields would then move from low to medium/high
  confidence.
- **Atmospheric spectrum.** No transmission or emission spectrum
  exists. A future JWST or direct-imaging spectrum would replace the
  tie-break composition and cloud tints with measured values.
- **Mass alternative.** The Vogt 2015 Msini = 21.3 M⊕ (recommended) vs
  Motalebi 2015 Msini = 8.67 M⊕ difference is large; if a future RV
  reanalysis resolves it, the sub-Neptune-vs-super-Earth character of
  d (and its radius estimate) should be revisited.
