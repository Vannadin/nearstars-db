# HD 219134 — Phase 3 Synthesis

HD 219134 (HR 8832, GJ 892, HIP 114622) is a bright, quiet K3 V dwarf
at 6.542 ± 0.002 pc (Gaia DR3 parallax 152.864 ± 0.049 mas, or
21.33 light-years) in Cassiopeia. At V = 5.53 it is one of the very
few exoplanet-host K dwarfs visible to the naked eye. Its fundamental
parameters are anchored on Ligi et al. 2019, a dedicated stellar-
parameters paper that directly resolved the star with VEGA/CHARA
visible-wavelength interferometry: θ_LD = 1.035 ± 0.021 mas →
R = 0.726 ± 0.014 R☉, giving Teff = 4858 ± 50 K (θ_LD + bolometric
flux), L = 0.264 ± 0.004 L☉, and a direct mass M = 0.696 ± 0.078 M☉
(from the interferometric radius combined with the transit-derived
stellar density). This makes HD 219134 an orange dwarf about 73% of
the Sun's radius and a quarter of its luminosity. The star is
chromospherically quiet — mean log R'HK = −4.89 (Johnson 2016) — and
carries a well-measured 11.7-year (4230 ± 100 d) S-index magnetic
activity cycle (Johnson 2016), an unusually clean Sun-like cycle for a
K dwarf.

The system is one of the richest nearby super-Earth families: two
**transiting** rocky super-Earths (b at 3.09 d, c at 6.77 d; both
characterized by Gillon et al. 2017 Spitzer photometry), three to four
RV-only warmer planets (d, f, g), and a **cold Saturn-class gas giant
h** at ~3.1 AU (Vogt et al. 2015). The transiting pair gives the rare
luxury of measured masses *and* radii — b is 4.74 M⊕ / 1.602 R⊕ and
c is 4.36 M⊕ / 1.511 R⊕, both denser than Earth (6.4 and 7.0 g/cc),
placing them among the highest-density known transiting super-Earths.
Planets f and g carry a controversy flag (`pl_controv_flag = 1`): f's
22.8-day period coincides with one of the candidate stellar rotation
periods (Johnson 2016's 22.83 d S_HK signal), so part of its RV
amplitude could be activity rather than a planet. NearStars keeps both
f and g for gameplay variety, flagged as contested.

**Scenario choice for NearStars: a bright, quiet orange K3 V dwarf
with a clean Sun-like 11.7-year activity cycle, hosting six planets
spanning hot dense rocky super-Earths, warm RV-only super-Earths /
mini-Neptunes, and a cold Saturn-class outer giant.** The stellar
layer is anchored on Ligi 2019 VEGA/CHARA interferometry (R, Teff, L,
direct mass) and Johnson 2016 activity/rotation. The rotation period
is genuinely contested (22.83 d Johnson 2016 vs 42.3 d Motalebi 2015);
the DB already adopts 22.83 d as recommended, documented in the
rotation synthesis below. One tie-break sets the visual surface tint
for the 4858 K K3 V SED; the spot-coverage and corona-extent rows sit
at low confidence. No circumstellar disk is known — no infrared-excess
detection or resolved imaging has been reported, so the star is
rendered diskless (search-and-verify policy; none fabricated).

## Surface synthesis

HD 219134 has the warm orange photosphere of an early-mid K dwarf. At
Teff = 4858 ± 50 K (Ligi 2019, interferometric F_bol + θ_LD) the SED
peaks in the red-orange near ~0.60 μm, and the optical spectrum is
beginning to show the molecular-band structure (TiO, CaH, the Ca I
4226 Å line strengthening) that becomes dominant only in the cooler M
dwarfs — at K3 the continuum is still mostly an orange blackbody with
strong metal lines rather than the heavily band-strangled spectrum of
an M dwarf. The interferometric radius R = 0.726 ± 0.014 R☉ and
luminosity L = 0.264 ± 0.004 L☉ make the star about 73% of the Sun's
size and a quarter of its luminosity, comfortably naked-eye at
V = 5.53 because of its proximity (6.5 pc).

The star has a direct interferometric angular diameter, which is the
anchor for the whole stellar layer: VEGA/CHARA visible-wavelength
interferometry resolved θ_LD = 1.035 ± 0.021 mas (Ligi 2019). This is
smaller than the earlier CHARA-Classic value (Boyajian 2012b
θ = 1.106 ± 0.007 mas) because VEGA reaches higher spatial frequencies,
and it supersedes the prior DB unverified 0.778 R☉. The direct mass
M = 0.696 ± 0.078 M☉ is itself a measurement — Ligi combine the
interferometric radius with the transit-derived stellar density
ρ = 1.82 ± 0.19 ρ☉ (from the b/c transit fits, Gillon 2017) rather
than relying on an evolutionary model; it is recommended over the
Motalebi 2015 SYCLIST evolutionary value 0.78 ± 0.02 M☉, with the two
agreeing at ~1.5σ.

Spot coverage is modest. The mean chromospheric index log R'HK = −4.89
(Johnson 2016) places HD 219134 just below the active/inactive
boundary (~−4.75) — a quiet but not dead K dwarf, livelier than the
very inactive solar twins (61 Vir at −5.01, τ Ceti) but far calmer
than a young active dwarf. The defining activity feature is a clean,
Sun-like **11.7-year magnetic cycle** (4230 ± 100 d; Johnson 2016) seen
in the Ca II S-index — an unusually well-resolved analog of the Sun's
~11-yr cycle, rare for a K dwarf. The cfg renders a modest cool-spot
filling factor (~2% at cycle maximum) that waxes and wanes on the
11.7-yr cycle, plus the underlying rotational modulation (the
contested 22.8 d / 42.3 d period; see Rotation & spin).

No metallicity is curated in the Phase 2 layer and none is synthesized
here; at fixed Teff the metallicity effect on the rendered K-dwarf
color is sub-perceptible, and project policy skips it for new hosts.
There is no debris disk: no infrared-excess detection or resolved
imaging of a disk around HD 219134 has been reported, so the star is
rendered diskless. The visual orange tint `#f0a868` sits between Sol's
near-white `#fff8f0` and the deeper orange-red of an M dwarf,
encoding the 4858 K photosphere as the warm-orange K3 V anchor of the
NearStars catalog.

## Atmosphere synthesis

A main-sequence star has no "atmosphere" in the planetary cfg sense;
for a stellar synthesis this section covers the chromosphere, corona,
and magnetic activity cycle.

HD 219134's chromosphere and corona are those of a quiet, mature K
dwarf. The mean Ca II H&K chromospheric index log R'HK = −4.89
(Johnson 2016) places it just inside the inactive locus. The headline
feature is the **magnetic activity cycle**: Johnson 2016 measure a
clean 11.7-year (4230 ± 100 d) periodicity in the Ca II S-index with a
well-defined amplitude (S_HK amplitude 0.0077 ± 0.0003), a remarkably
Sun-like cycle for a K dwarf. This cycle, not a single static activity
level, is what the cfg should animate — a slow waxing and waning of
the chromospheric brightness and spot coverage over the 11.7-yr
period, superimposed on the faster rotational modulation.

No long-baseline X-ray cycle has been published, and the DB carries no
X-ray luminosity measurement, so the cfg does not synthesize an
explicit log L_X range. For a quiet K3 V at log R'HK = −4.89 the
expected coronal output is modestly above the very quiet G/K dwarfs
but well below an active dwarf — a benign space-weather environment for
the close-in transiting planets b and c, whose airless / thin-
atmosphere states are set by insolation and escape rather than by an
aggressive stellar wind.

The corona/chromosphere visual extent is rendered at the quiet-Sun
reference — a thin chromospheric layer at ~1.005 R★ and a faint corona
extending to ~3 R★ visible only during the in-game "corona-on"
rendering mode. The integrated XUV luminosity stays well below
10⁻⁴ L_bol; it matters for the close-in planets' atmospheric retention
but is treated in their respective Phase 3 syntheses.

## Rotation & spin synthesis

The rotation period of HD 219134 is genuinely contested, and the cfg
carries the contest explicitly. The Phase 2 recommended value is
**P_rot = 22.83 ± 0.03 d** (Johnson 2016), from a sinusoid fit to the
post-2007 Ca II S_HK time series. But this is not a photometric period
— Vogt 2015's APT photometry is flat to ~0.0002 mag, so no clean
brightness-modulation period exists, and the value carries
`method = unverified` (the escape hatch for a real technique outside
the rotation enum). Two alternatives are documented in the DB:
Motalebi 2015 found **42.3 d** from GLS periodograms of log R'HK, the
CCF FWHM, and the bisector span (HARPS-N); Vogt 2015 estimated **~20 d**
from v sin i = 1.8 km/s. Johnson explicitly note that the 22.83 d
signal may be the first harmonic of a ~45 d period under solar-like
differential rotation — i.e. the 22.83 d and 42.3 d values may be the
same physical rotation seen at the fundamental and its first harmonic.

The 22.83 d period has a specific downstream consequence: it nearly
coincides with planet f's 22.8 d orbital period (Vogt 2015), which is
exactly why f is flagged `pl_controv_flag = 1` — part of f's RV
amplitude could be a stellar-activity signal at the rotation period
rather than a genuine planet. NearStars keeps f for gameplay variety
(flagged contested in the f synthesis), but the rotation/planet
coincidence is the reason for the flag.

**KSP implementation note.** Stellar rotation period = 22.83 d =
1 972 512 s. In Kopernicus the star body's `rotationPeriod` is set in
seconds. Kopernicus does not animate stellar rotation directly, but the
`rotationPeriod` field is read by Principia for spin-axis bookkeeping;
the contested value is carried at medium confidence with the 42.3 d
alternative documented in Open items.

The rotation-axis inclination is unconstrained. The v sin i = 1.8 km/s
(Vogt 2015) is consistent with both the 22.8 d and 42.3 d periods
depending on the inclination, so it does not break the degeneracy. For
NearStars visual rendering a generic axis orientation is adopted; the
slow-to-moderate rotation makes the precise spin axis visually
unimportant for the photosphere, mattering only for the timing of the
spot-modulation animation.

## Visual styling

In the NearStars renderer, HD 219134 is portrayed as a bright, warm
orange K3 V dwarf with a clean Sun-like activity cycle:

- **Global appearance.** A warm orange disk encoded as `#f0a868`, the
  4858 K blackbody continuum with the first onset of TiO band
  suppression of the blue — clearly warmer/redder than a G dwarf, but
  well short of the deep orange-red of an M dwarf. The illumination
  color temperature for scene lighting of the six planets is driven
  directly by the 4858 K SED.
- **Spotted surface with an 11.7-yr cycle.** A modest cool-spot
  filling factor (`visual_spot_coverage_max ≈ 0.02`) that waxes and
  wanes over the well-measured 11.7-year magnetic cycle (Johnson 2016),
  superimposed on the faster rotational modulation (22.8 d / 42.3 d
  contested). The cfg should animate the slow cycle as the primary
  activity signature — distinctive because the cycle is so cleanly
  Sun-like for a K dwarf.
- **Quiet corona.** A thin chromospheric layer and a faint corona to
  ~3 R★ in the "corona-on" rendering mode. No prominent flares or CMEs
  — HD 219134 is a calm host.
- **Star in the planets' skies.** The star is dominant in the inner
  planets' skies: from b (a = 0.0382 AU) it subtends ~10.1° (about 20×
  the Sun's angular size from Earth); from c (~6.0°), f (~2.7°), d
  (~1.6°), g (~1.0°), and from the cold giant h (~0.12°, just larger
  than the Sun from Earth). The disk is warm orange and floods the
  close-in rocky worlds with red-rich light.
- **From Earth.** HD 219134 is a V = 5.53 naked-eye star in Cassiopeia
  — one of the very few K-dwarf exoplanet hosts visible without a
  telescope, thanks to its 21.3-light-year proximity. In NearStars'
  real-stars-only mode it appears at its Gaia DR3 J2016.0 sky position
  propagated linearly to the game epoch.

## Open items for follow-up

- **Rotation period resolution.** The 22.83 d (Johnson 2016) vs 42.3 d
  (Motalebi 2015) contest is unresolved; the cfg carries 22.83 d
  (Phase 2 recommended) at medium confidence with 42.3 d documented as
  the alternative. A future photometric rotation detection (TESS, or
  long-baseline ground photometry beating the ~0.0002 mag APT floor)
  would break the harmonic ambiguity and could shift the
  `rotation_period_days` value and the f-controversy assessment.
- **Age constraint.** No Phase 2 age array (models span 0.2–13.8 Gyr).
  If a future curation adds a gyrochronology or asteroseismic age, the
  null `age_gyr` should be populated; the clean 11.7 yr cycle and
  log R'HK = −4.89 point to an intermediate-to-old quiet K dwarf.
- **X-ray / XUV monitoring.** No frozen X-ray luminosity in the DB; a
  Chandra/XMM or eROSITA detection would let the cfg attach a
  quantitative coronal output and refine the close-in planets'
  atmospheric-escape narratives.
- **Planet f / g confirmation.** Both carry `pl_controv_flag = 1`; f's
  period coincides with the 22.83 d rotation signal. If future RV +
  activity-indicator analysis confirms or retracts either, the planet
  set should be revisited (currently both kept for gameplay variety).
- **Disk non-detection confirmation.** No IR-excess search result is
  curated; a Herschel/Spitzer/WISE non-detection (or detection) would
  formalize the `disk_present = false` decision now adopted on the
  search-and-verify "none reported" basis.
