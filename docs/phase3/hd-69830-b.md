# HD 69830 b — Phase 3 Synthesis

HD 69830 b is the innermost of three Neptune-mass radial-velocity
planets discovered by Lovis et al. 2006 (HARPS, 4-year campaign):
P = 8.667 ± 0.003 d, Msini = 10.2 M⊕, a = 0.0785 AU, e = 0.10. With
the host star at L = 0.60 L☉ this places b at ~97× Earth's
insolation and an equilibrium temperature of 800 K (A = 0.3 Bond
albedo) — hot enough that any primordial H/He envelope is on the
margin of photoevaporative survival over the system's 5.7 ± 1 Gyr
age. No transit detection: Bonfils 2007 and subsequent monitoring
exclude grazing geometries, so only Msini and the orbital elements
are observationally constrained. Atmospheric characterisation is
not possible — neither transit spectroscopy nor direct imaging
reaches the required mass / contrast at HD 69830's 12.58 pc
distance with current facilities.

**Scenario choice for NearStars: a hot, tidally-locked stripped
sub-Neptune — the photoevaporation-survivor scenario from Owen &
Wu 2017 in which the primordial H/He envelope has been largely
eroded, leaving a heavy-element-rich (water + rock) core with at
most a thin (~1–3% by mass) retained envelope.** 28 cfg picks;
12 canonical-aligned (orbital + mass parameters, derived
quantities), 16 tie-break (radius / density across the stripped-core
vs. retained-envelope window, surface visuals, atmosphere chemistry
under photoevaporation). No documented divergences — Lovis 2006
treats the bare-core possibility explicitly in §5 and the cfg picks
the stripped-core reading from within the observation-allowed
window.

## Surface synthesis

HD 69830 b has no surface in the geological sense. Under the
adopted stripped-sub-Neptune scenario, the visible "surface" — at
the optical depth where photons escape the planet — is a
photochemical haze deck high in the residual H/He + H₂O envelope,
roughly 100 bar above any rocky / icy interior. The deeper
core-envelope boundary lies at supercritical conditions: T > 1500 K
at the rock surface for a partially-retained envelope, well above
the silicate melt line for a substellar dome. The geological
"surface" is therefore largely irrelevant to the player view; what
matters is the haze layer chemistry and the column distribution.

The stripped-sub-Neptune scenario follows Owen & Wu 2017's
photoevaporation-survivor framework. For a 10.2 M⊕ planet at 0.078 AU
around an old G8V, the XUV-driven envelope mass-loss timescale at
~1 Gyr is short enough to strip most of a Saturn-mass primordial
envelope, leaving a heavy-element-rich core with at most a few
percent residual envelope by mass — exactly the "photoevaporation
desert survivor" locus identified by Fulton 2017 (Kepler radius gap)
and modelled in detail by Rogers 2021. This is a tie-break against
the alternative "envelope-retaining warm Neptune" reading; the cfg
defaults to stripped because (a) the host star age 5.7 Gyr gives the
photoevaporation engine plenty of time to act, (b) 10.2 M⊕ is below
the "photoevaporation valley floor" mass of ~15 M⊕ where envelopes
survive comfortably, and (c) the stripped scenario produces a more
visually distinctive contrast against c and d (which retain their
envelopes).

The conceptual rocky / icy interior is encoded for the cfg-variant
renderer that strips the haze: a warm dark-brown silicate /
water-rock crust (`#7a4a30`) with a substellar partial-melt accent
(`#a85a30`) where dayside heat from above and tidal/radiogenic heat
from below combine to push the upper mantle above the silicate
solidus. This is purely a tie-break interesting-first visual — no
observation constrains the interior geometry. Below the crust, the
interior is expected to be a "wet Earth" composition with a few
percent water by mass in the silicate matrix, rather than a fully
ice-dominated Uranus-analog mantle. The host star's slightly
metal-poor [Fe/H] = −0.05 has minimal effect on core composition at
this metallicity.

Under the alternative envelope-retaining scenario (cfg variant), b
would appear as a small uniform warm-tan disk much like the
photochemical haze of the stripped reading but with no visible
surface at any depth — the envelope continuously transitions from
H₂-rich gas to supercritical fluid without a phase boundary the
renderer can resolve. The two cfg variants are visually almost
indistinguishable from orbit; the difference matters only for the
interior cutaway view.

## Atmosphere synthesis

The atmospheric residue is the defining observable of b under the
stripped-sub-Neptune cfg. Following Owen 2019 §4 and Rogers 2021,
the residual envelope after photoevaporation is dominated by H₂
(80%) and He (15%), with H₂O (3%) and trace heavy elements (CH₄, NH₃,
Na, K) supplied by the underlying core through long-timescale
upward mixing. At 800 K equilibrium and 100 bar column, the upper
atmosphere photochemistry follows Morley 2015 / Kawashima 2019 hot
sub-Neptune models: methane photolysis produces hydrocarbon soot
particles (a Titan-analog photochemical haze) at altitudes of
0.1–1 mbar, with optical depth ~5 in the visible band. This haze
deck is the photometric surface of b.

The cfg adopts a thick global photochemical haze (`cloud_cover_fraction
= 0.90`) covering essentially the entire disk, with weak day-night
contrast. Tidal locking means the atmospheric circulation should
develop a substellar-to-antistellar overturn — Showman & Polvani
2011 jets at the relevant 8.67 d rotation period yield a Kelvin /
Rossby wave pattern that piles haze on the eastward terminator and
slightly thins it at the substellar point. The cfg encodes this as
a uniform tan deck with subtle eastward-skewed brightening, rather
than as a sharply differentiated dayside / nightside contrast.

The atmospheric tint is `#a07050` warm amber — Titan-haze tan
modulated by G8V (rather than Sol) illumination. The host star is
slightly cooler (5385 K vs Sol's 5772 K) so the perceived hue is
shifted ~5 K toward red, marginally warming the tan reference. The
cloud / haze tint `#c89070` is a slightly lighter version of the
same palette, used for the upper haze deck visible from orbit.

**Sky from the substellar region (if a hypothetical lander descended
through the haze).** Above the haze deck, the star fills a 5.4° disk
in the sky — about 10× the apparent Sun-from-Earth diameter — and
its slightly redder-than-Sol cream-yellow tint floods the entire
upper atmosphere. Below the haze (at deeper pressure), photons no
longer reach from outside; the planet is its own thermal source, and
a hypothetical instrument at 100 bar would see a dim red-orange
thermal background corresponding to the ~1100 K substellar temperature.
No nightside sky distinction in the conventional sense — the haze
deck and the dense lower atmosphere completely thermalise.

## Rotation & spin synthesis

Tidal damping for b at 8.67-day orbital period over 5.7 ± 1 Gyr
establishes the synchronous (1:1) configuration: the
synchronisation timescale for a Neptune-class planet at 0.08 AU
around an old G-dwarf is ~10⁶ years (Heller 2011 scaling from
Mercury-rocky to Neptune-class moment of inertia), comfortably below
the system age. Obliquity damped to zero by the same dissipation
mechanism over the same timescale.

The orbital eccentricity 0.10 is non-negligible — large enough that
a 3:2 spin-orbit resonance is dynamically plausible (Vinson 2017's
e > 0.1 threshold is approached but not exceeded). The cfg adopts
the 1:1 synchronous state as the canonical Neptune-class reading
(consistent with all observed close-in Neptune-mass planets without
direct rotation measurements), but the 3:2 alternative is
preserved as a cfg variant in Open items — under 3:2, the
substellar point would migrate around the planet every ~4.3 days
in the inertial frame, producing a slow but resolvable
substellar-spot drift in the haze morphology.

**KSP implementation note.** Rotation period = orbital period =
8.667 days (749 088 s) for the canonical synchronous cfg. Under the
3:2 cfg variant, rotation period = (2/3) × orbital period = 5.78 days
(499 392 s).

**Eccentric-orbit insolation cycling.** At e = 0.10, the
star-planet distance varies between 0.071 AU (perihelion) and
0.086 AU (aphelion) over each 8.67-day orbit, with insolation
cycling between 118 S⊕ (perihelion) and 81 S⊕ (aphelion). The
~45% insolation amplitude drives a thermal-tide forcing on the
upper atmosphere; combined with the modest internal tidal heating
(~0.01 W/m², Bolmont 2013 scaling for Neptune-class Q at e = 0.10),
the perihelion-aphelion temperature swing is expected to be ~50 K
peak-to-peak at the substellar haze deck. The cfg does not animate
this; it is informational for the deeper cfg interior model.

## Visual styling

- **Global appearance.** A small warm-tan haze disk (`#c89070` to
  `#a07050` gradient with limb darkening) about 0.04° apparent
  diameter from a viewpoint at HD 69830 c — much smaller than the
  host star's 5.4° angular diameter at b's own distance from the
  star. Player view from outside b shows a uniform Titan-coloured
  disk with no resolved structure at orbit-view scale.
- **Dayside detail.** Faint substellar dome brightening (~10% over
  the global tan) from haze chemistry compression at the warmest
  point. The dayside ranges from `#c89070` near the substellar
  point to `#a07050` at the terminator.
- **Terminator band.** Soft transition from amber to dim brown
  (`#704830`) with no sharp edge — a Neptune-analog haze
  scattering profile rather than the sharp terminator of an
  airless body.
- **Nightside.** Dim red-brown glow from residual thermal
  emission of the 600 K nightside haze deck. Visible only in cfg
  variants with night-side IR rendering enabled.
- **Atmosphere haze.** The haze IS the surface. No discrete
  cloud features beyond the global deck; no banding visible at
  the cfg's resolution. Limb glow on the dayside crescent gives a
  ~5° angular feathered edge.
- **Star in sky.** HD 69830 fills 5.39° in b's sky — about 10× the
  Sun's apparent diameter from Earth, an enormous dim red-orange
  disk dominating the daytime hemisphere. The illumination is
  cream-amber, slightly redder than Sol.
- **Sister planets in sky.** From b, planet c appears at angular
  diameter 0.025° during close conjunctions (a/c separation 0.11 AU
  at conjunction; c's radius ~22 000 km), and d at 0.005° at its
  closest. Far more visible features in b's sky are the warm dust
  ring at 0.8–1.5 AU, seen in projection as a faint amber band
  across the sky during alignment events.
- **Warm dust ring from b's perspective.** The dust belt at ~1 AU
  is exterior to b's 0.08 AU orbit by a factor of 13. From b, the
  belt is seen as a faint band across the orbital plane —
  comparable to viewing the Sun's zodiacal light from inside
  Mercury's orbit. Brightness peaks during perihelion-of-belt
  alignment events but stays well below the host star's overwhelming
  glow.

## Open items for follow-up

- **Transit search refresh.** No published transit detection as of
  2026; CHEOPS / TESS short-cadence monitoring could rule in or
  out a grazing geometry and pin a true radius. If a transit is
  detected, the stripped-core vs envelope-retaining ambiguity
  resolves immediately, and the cfg `radius_rearth` should be
  updated from the tie-break 2.6 R⊕.
- **Envelope-retaining cfg variant.** The "warm Neptune with retained
  primordial envelope" reading (radius 3.17 R⊕, density ~1.7 g/cc,
  Neptune-analog atmospheric composition) is preserved as a cfg
  variant. Visual difference from the stripped reading is small at
  orbit-view scale; the interior cutaway view differs significantly.
- **3:2 spin-orbit cfg variant.** At e = 0.10, the 3:2 alternative
  is dynamically plausible (Makarov 2018). A 5.78-day rotation
  period would produce a slow substellar drift visible as a
  migrating haze brightening — different gameplay feel than the
  static synchronous lock.
- **Direct atmospheric characterisation.** No JWST programme has
  targeted HD 69830 b through 2026 cycles (HD 69830 is too quiet /
  not transiting for the standard JWST sub-Neptune programmes).
  A future direct-imaging facility (HabEx, LUVOIR, ELT/HARMONI)
  could resolve b at angular separation 6 milliarcsec — within
  ELT capability for a planet/star contrast ~10⁻⁹. The cfg's haze
  chemistry would be testable.
- **Tidal heating cross-check.** The adopted 0.01 W/m² internal
  heat flux from e = 0.10 + Neptune-class Q is order-of-magnitude;
  more rigorous tidal-evolution modelling (Bolmont 2013-style for
  the specific HD 69830 b parameters) would refine this and could
  feed back into the cfg's interior cutaway view.
