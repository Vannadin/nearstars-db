# PSR J0108−1431 — Phase 3 Synthesis

PSR J0108−1431 is the nearest known radio pulsar to the Sun — a
solitary neutron star in the constellation Cetus, discovered in the
1994 Parkes Southern Pulsar Survey (Tauris et al. 1994, who titled the
discovery paper "the closest known neutron star?"). It sits **far
beyond the 50 ly Kopernicus cap** at roughly 210–240 pc (~685 ly), so
it is curated as a beyond-implementation-range data / Phase-3 entry,
not a placeable NearStars body. It is included because it is the
single most extreme stellar remnant in our neighbourhood and a clean
test of how the pipeline handles a compact object.

The distance is genuinely contested. The smallest dispersion measure
of any radio pulsar (DM ≈ 2.38 pc cm⁻³) first flagged it as nearby
(~85–130 pc), but the VLBI parallax of Deller et al. 2009
(π = 4.17 ± 1.42 mas) placed it almost twice as far — 240 +124/−61 pc
raw, revised to ~210 pc after the Lutz–Kelker bias correction of
Verbiest et al. 2012. The DB stores the direct VLBI parallax (→ 240
pc); the LK-corrected 210 pc is the better population estimate. Either
way the legacy ~130 pc figure that still circulates is superseded.

It is an **ordinary (non-recycled), old, faint pulsar** sitting close
to the radio "death line": spin period 0.8076 s, characteristic age
~168 Myr, surface dipole field ~2.5×10¹¹ G. The same VLBI campaign
measured a large proper motion (~170 mas/yr → transverse velocity
~194 km/s), the supernova-kick signature of its birth. It is one of
the least X-ray-luminous pulsars detected, seen faintly by both
Chandra and XMM-Newton. A dedicated near-IR companion search
(Posselt et al. 2008/09) found nothing to ~5000 AU: **this is a
solitary neutron star with no planets.**

**Scenario choice for NearStars: a planetless stellar corpse — an
~12 km, ~1.4 M☉ sphere spinning every 0.8 s, optically invisible
(V ≳ 26), detectable only as a radio/X-ray point and a sweeping
magnetospheric beam.** The standard stellar Phase 3 fields (Teff-driven
surface tint, chromospheric activity) do not apply; the synthesis is
built from the compact-object physics instead. Every "measured" row
that depends on the mass or radius is flagged low confidence because
no neutron-star mass or radius has actually been measured for this
object — the canonical 1.4 M☉ / 12 km are assumptions, not data.

## Surface

A neutron star has no photosphere in the stellar sense. Its ~12 km
solid/ionised crust radiates thermally in the soft X-ray band: the
blackbody fits imply a temperature of order 1–3 million K
(kT ≈ 0.11–0.28 keV) over an emitting area far smaller than the whole
surface — tens of square metres, i.e. a **polar cap** (R_bb ≈ 40 m,
Posselt 2012), heated by return currents from the magnetosphere rather
than the bulk surface. In optical light the object is dead: at ~210 pc
a 12 km thermal source is fainter than V ≈ 26, well below any survey.
So the honest "surface tint" is *no visible surface at all*; if the
renderer must place a point, a hot blue-white (`#cfe0ff`) matches the
X-ray/UV-peaked spectrum.

## Magnetosphere & emission

This is where everything interesting lives — the analogue of the
"atmosphere" section for a normal star. A ~2.5×10¹¹ G dipole and a
0.8 s rotation power a magnetosphere that accelerates particles along
open field lines, producing the radio beam that lets us see the
object at all. The spin-down luminosity (~5.8×10³⁰ erg/s) is the
energy budget; only a tiny fraction emerges as the faint X-rays.
Sitting near the death line, its radio emission is intrinsically weak
— it was the faintest-known radio pulsar at discovery. If visualised
(cf. the planned flux-tube/magnetosphere renderer), the signature
elements are the twin emission cones sweeping past the line of sight
once per 0.8 s and a faint hot polar cap, not a glowing globe.

## Rotation & spin-down

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Spin period | 0.8076 s |
| Spin-down age | ~168 Myr (P/2Ṗ) |
| Field | ~2.5×10¹¹ G |
| State | non-recycled, near the radio death line |

The slow period and old characteristic age mark it as a pulsar near
the end of its radio-active life — not a millisecond pulsar (no
recycling by accretion, consistent with its solitude). The large
space velocity (~194 km/s) is the fossil of an asymmetric supernova
kick ~10⁸ yr ago.

## Visual styling

Optically invisible is the correct default. For a data-visualisation
or future renderer, the object is best represented as a sub-pixel
blue-white point (`#cfe0ff`) with an optional swept twin beam keyed to
the 0.8 s spin, and — at X-ray wavelengths only — a faint hot polar
cap. There is no disc, no companion, no planet, and no extended
atmosphere to style. The high proper motion means that over long
in-game timescales it would visibly drift against the background.

## Open items for follow-up

- **Distance** remains the dominant uncertainty (parallax only ~3σ; 130 → 240 → 210 pc history). The DB keeps the raw VLBI parallax; revisit if a more precise parallax appears.
- **Ṗ / τ / Ė / B** carry a factor-~2 discrepancy between the Pavlov literature and the ATNF timing solution — the report adopts the Pavlov set; flagged in compact_object.notes.
- **X-ray nature** (thermal polar cap vs magnetospheric) is unresolved at current photon counts.
- **No mass/radius measurement** exists; the canonical values are placeholders. A future NICER-class result or a fortunate binary would upgrade these from "assumed" to "measured."
