# HD 69830 c — Phase 3 Synthesis

HD 69830 c is the middle of three Neptune-mass radial-velocity
planets discovered by Lovis et al. 2006 (HARPS, 4-year campaign):
P = 31.56 ± 0.04 d, Msini = 11.8–12.1 M⊕ (Lovis 2006 / Tanner 2015
refit), a = 0.186 AU, e = 0.13, ω = 221°. With the host star at
L = 0.622 L☉ (Tanner 2015) this places c at ~18× Earth's
insolation and an equilibrium temperature of 524 K (A = 0.3 Bond
albedo) — warm
enough that a primordial Neptune-class H/He envelope is
comfortably retained over the 5.7 ± 1 Gyr system age. No transit
detection: Bonfils 2007 and follow-up photometry exclude the
predicted ephemeris, so only Msini and the orbital elements are
observationally constrained. The semi-major axis 0.186 AU places c
~3.8× interior to the warm dust belt inner edge at 0.7–0.8 AU
(Beichman 2005), so c orbits firmly inside the dust population
without crossing it.

**Scenario choice for NearStars: a warm envelope-retaining Neptune
analog — sub-Neptune to mini-Neptune class with a thick H₂/He
atmosphere ~25% of total mass, a water-rich icy interior, and a
chromophore-bearing photochemical haze layer giving the planet its
defining visual signature.** 28 cfg picks; 12 canonical-aligned
(orbital + mass parameters, derived insolation / equilibrium
temperature), 16 tie-break (radius / density in the
envelope-retaining mini-Neptune window, photochemical chromophore
tint, cloud morphology under modest tidal-spin coupling). No
documented divergences — the envelope-retaining reading is the
canonical interpretation for a 12 M⊕ planet at 17 S⊕ around an
old G8V, sitting comfortably above the photoevaporation valley
floor and within the Rogers 2021 envelope-retention locus.

## Surface synthesis

HD 69830 c has no surface in the geological sense. The
envelope-retaining mini-Neptune scenario places c firmly above
the photoevaporation valley: at 12.1 M⊕ and 18.0 S⊕, c sits
comfortably inside the envelope-retention locus of Fulton 2017
and Rogers 2021 — well above the ~5 M⊕ threshold where XUV-driven
mass-loss strips a substantial envelope over Gyr timescales, even
for an old G-dwarf host. The cfg adopts the canonical Neptune-analog
interior structure: a small (~3 M⊕) rocky/iron core, surrounded by
a deep (~6 M⊕) supercritical H₂O / NH₃ / CH₄ ice mantle, with a
thick (~3 M⊕, ~25% of total mass) H₂/He envelope on top. The
"surface" the renderer sees is the cloud / haze deck at ~0.1 bar in
the upper troposphere.

The envelope-retention reading is canonical at this point in the
mass-insolation diagram — Lovis 2006 §5's bare-core ambiguity that
mattered for b (10 M⊕, 97 S⊕, marginal evaporation locus) does not
apply to c. The photoevaporation timescale at 0.186 AU around an
old G8V is several Gyr longer than at b's 0.078 AU, and the
host XUV luminosity (log L_X ≈ 26.7, Merloni 2024) is too feeble
to drive significant mass-loss from a 12 M⊕ envelope over the
system age. The cfg therefore picks the canonical envelope-retaining
Neptune without a stripped-core variant.

The conceptual interior color for the cfg-variant cutaway renderer
encodes a Neptune-analog deep mantle: `#5a7060` deep teal-grey for
the supercritical ice mantle dominant body, with `#80a890` warmer
green-teal accents where convective plumes from the rocky core
bring warmer material toward the envelope base. This is purely
tie-break for the cfg's interior-cutaway view; no observation
constrains the deep-interior geometry. Below the supercritical-ice
mantle, the inner rocky/iron core composition follows the
metallicity scaling of the host star ([Fe/H] = −0.05), giving a
slightly Fe-deficient core relative to Sol-analog assumptions but
within the population-typical envelope.

The envelope's deep base reaches ~3000 K at the ice-mantle / envelope
boundary (pressure ~50 kbar, far below visible-photon escape depths).
The renderer's interior cutaway view shows the layered structure
schematically — there is no observed property that constrains the
internal energy budget beyond the modest tidal heating (~0.002 W/m²)
from the e = 0.13 eccentricity, well below Neptune's own intrinsic
heat flux (~0.4 W/m²) and therefore subdominant to primordial heat
of formation and radiogenic decay.

## Atmosphere synthesis

The atmosphere is c's defining observable. Following Moses 2013
and Madhusudhan 2016 for warm-Neptune chemistry, the 520 K
equilibrium temperature places c in the "warm Neptune" regime
where CH₄ is the dominant carbon carrier (vs CO at hotter T > 1000 K),
NH₃ is stable in the lower stratosphere (vs N₂ at hotter T),
and H₂O condensation is suppressed in favor of supercritical mixing
at the envelope base. The visible cloud deck at ~0.1 bar comprises
NH₃ ice / hydrosulfide (NH₄SH) cloud particles plus a
photochemical sulfur-bearing chromophore haze in the upper
stratosphere, analogous to the bright olive / yellow tints of
Saturn's photochemical hazes at mid-latitudes.

The cfg adopts a banded zonal flow morphology
(`cloud_cover_fraction = 0.70`) with weak substellar / antistellar
asymmetry. The pseudo-synchronous rotation state at P_rot ≈ 0.84
P_orb (Hut 1981, Greenberg 2009 e-dependent equilibrium) gives c
a rotation period of ~26.5 days — slow enough that the
Showman & Polvani 2011 zonal-jet morphology develops modest
banding but not the strong Saturn-Jupiter contrast. The cfg
encodes ~3 alternating chromophore-rich (darker olive-gold) and
chromophore-poor (lighter olive-cream) bands across the mid-latitudes,
with a weaker tropical / polar contrast.

The atmospheric tint is `#a8b885` warm olive-gold — Sudarsky 2003's
Class III ("warm Neptune") palette with the photochemical
chromophore contribution from Moses 2013's sulfur-bearing
hydrocarbon haze at 520 K. The host star is slightly cooler than
Sol (5402 K vs 5772 K) so the perceived hue is shifted toward
warmer/redder, but the dominant signal is the atmospheric
chromophore chemistry rather than the stellar illumination. The
cloud tint `#c8d4a0` is the brighter zones of the banded structure,
visible at orbital scale as alternating belt-zone striping.

**Sky from the substellar region.** Above the cloud deck, the star
fills a 2.3° disk in the sky — about 4× the apparent Sun-from-Earth
diameter — and its slightly redder-than-Sol cream-yellow tint
modulates the upper atmosphere. The warm dust belt at 0.7–1.5 AU
is exterior to c by a factor of 4–8, and from c's vantage projects
as a faint amber band across the sky during alignment events,
distinctly brighter than from b but still well below the host
star's overwhelming glow. The sister planet d at 0.63 AU appears
at a 0.03° angular diameter from c during opposition (closest
approach 0.44 AU), brighter than any star except HD 69830 itself.

## Rotation & spin synthesis

Tidal damping for c at 31.56-day orbital period over 5.7 ± 1 Gyr
does not reach full 1:1 synchronisation: the synchronisation
timescale for a Neptune-class planet at 0.186 AU around an old
G-dwarf is ≈ 10⁸ years (Heller 2011 scaling), comparable to but
not significantly shorter than the system age. The equilibrium
spin state for e = 0.13 is pseudo-synchronous (Hut 1981, Greenberg
2009), with rotation period P_rot ≈ 0.84 × P_orb = 26.5 days,
giving the substellar point a slow ~5°/day drift in the inertial
frame. Obliquity is partially damped to ~5° rather than the full
0° of a synchronous planet, preserved by the same e-driven
forcing that establishes the pseudo-sync state.

The pseudo-sync rotation rate is much slower than the strong
zonal-wind regime of close-in tidally-locked hot Jupiters
(Showman & Polvani 2011), but fast enough to drive equatorial
superrotation by a factor ~2–3× the rotation rate of the surface
frame. The resulting banded morphology is intermediate between
Saturn's strong belt-zone structure (3-day rotation) and Neptune's
weaker banding (16-hour rotation but cold) — c is closer to a
warm-Saturn than a Neptune-analog in its visible atmospheric
dynamics.

**KSP implementation note.** Rotation period = 26.5 days (2 289 600 s)
under the pseudo-sync cfg. A canonical-alternative cfg variant with
a free-rotator (P_rot ≈ 16 h, Neptune-analog) is preserved in Open
items; under that reading the banding would be sharper and the
substellar drift faster, but the equilibrium pseudo-sync state is
the dynamically expected configuration for an old planet at e = 0.13.

**Eccentric-orbit insolation cycling.** At e = 0.13, the
star-planet distance varies between 0.162 AU (perihelion) and
0.210 AU (aphelion) over each 31.56-day orbit, with insolation
cycling between 22.9 S⊕ (perihelion) and 13.6 S⊕ (aphelion). The
~50% insolation amplitude drives a thermal-tide forcing on the
upper atmosphere comparable to b's, but at 520 K rather than 800 K
the haze chemistry response is less dramatic — the cfg expects a
~30 K peak-to-peak swing at the upper cloud deck per orbit. Not
animated; informational for the deeper interior model.

## Visual styling

- **Global appearance.** A warm olive-gold disk (`#a8b885`
  central tint, `#c8d4a0` brighter zonal bands) about 0.04°
  apparent diameter from a viewpoint at HD 69830 b — comparable
  to b's apparent size from c. Player view from outside c shows
  banded olive-gold structure with ~3 chromophore-rich /
  chromophore-poor stripes resolvable at orbit-view scale, more
  visually distinctive than b's uniform haze disk.
- **Dayside detail.** Modest substellar dome brightening (~5% over
  the global tint) from chromophore chemistry at the warmest
  substellar point. The dayside ranges from `#c8d4a0` near the
  substellar point to `#a8b885` at the terminator.
- **Terminator band.** Gradient olive to dim olive-grey
  (`#5a6850`) with a soft Neptune-analog scattering profile, no
  sharp edge.
- **Nightside.** Dim olive-grey glow from residual thermal
  emission of the 470 K nightside cloud deck. Visible only in
  cfg variants with night-side IR rendering enabled.
- **Atmosphere & cloud structure.** The defining feature is the
  banded zonal flow. Chromophore-rich darker bands at low-mid
  latitudes alternate with brighter chromophore-poor zones,
  ~3 bands across the visible hemisphere at orbit scale. Bands
  drift slowly with the pseudo-sync rotation; subtle wave
  patterns visible on close inspection.
- **Star in sky.** HD 69830 fills 2.59° in c's sky — about 5× the
  Sun's apparent diameter from Earth, a large cream-yellow disk
  comparable to an apparent Sun seen from inside Mercury's orbit.
- **Sister planets in sky.** From c, planet b appears at angular
  diameter ~0.03° during conjunction (a/b separation 0.11 AU,
  b's haze-disk radius ~16 600 km), and d at ~0.04° during d's
  closest approach (a/d separation 0.44 AU, d's radius
  ~28 400 km). d is the brighter of the two from c's vantage
  because of its larger radius despite the greater distance.
- **Warm dust ring from c's perspective.** The dust belt at
  0.7–1.5 AU is exterior to c's 0.186 AU orbit by a factor of
  4–8. From c, the belt is seen as an amber band across the
  orbital plane — narrower and dimmer than from a hypothetical
  outside-belt observer, but distinctly brighter than from b
  (which is 13× interior to the belt). Brightness peaks during
  perihelion alignments but stays well below the host star.

## Open items for follow-up

- **Transit search refresh.** No published transit detection as of
  2026; CHEOPS / TESS short-cadence monitoring could rule in or
  out a grazing geometry and pin a true radius. A transit detection
  for c would resolve the mass-radius tie-break and update
  `radius_rearth` from 3.46 R⊕ to the measured value.
- **Pseudo-sync vs free-rotator cfg variant.** The 26.5-day
  pseudo-sync state is the dynamically expected equilibrium; a
  free-rotator alternative (P_rot ≈ 16 h, Neptune-analog) is
  preserved as a cfg variant. The visual difference is in the
  sharpness of the banded structure — free rotation gives
  sharper bands and faster substellar drift.
- **Direct atmospheric characterisation.** No JWST programme has
  targeted HD 69830 c through 2026 cycles. Future direct-imaging
  facilities (HabEx, LUVOIR, ELT/HARMONI) could resolve c at
  angular separation ~15 milliarcsec — within ELT capability for
  a planet/star contrast ~10⁻⁸. The cfg's banded chromophore
  morphology would be testable through reflected-light spectroscopy.
- **Resonance check vs b and d.** The b:c period ratio 31.56/8.667
  = 3.64 is far from 4:1 mean-motion resonance; the c:d ratio
  197/31.56 = 6.24 is also non-resonant. Long-term dynamical
  stability papers (Ji 2007, Payne 2009) confirm the system is
  stable in this non-resonant configuration. No cfg variant needed.
- **Tidal heating cross-check.** The adopted 0.002 W/m² internal
  heat flux from e = 0.13 + Neptune-class Q is order-of-magnitude;
  more rigorous tidal-evolution modelling (Bolmont 2013-style)
  would refine this.
