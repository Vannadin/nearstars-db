# HD 219134 g — Phase 3 Synthesis

HD 219134 g is an RV-only, **contested** planet of the bright quiet
K3 V dwarf HD 219134, on a 94.2-day orbit at a = 0.3753 AU. It does not
transit, so the only directly measured physical quantity is the minimum
mass: Msini = 10.8 ± 1.27 M⊕ (Vogt et al. 2015, recommended). Like f,
g carries a controversy flag (`pl_controv_flag = 1`) — it is one of the
two planets added in the Vogt 2015 six-planet solution that subsequent
work has questioned. NearStars keeps g for gameplay variety, flagged as
contested. It is the coolest of the inner planets: the equilibrium
temperature from the project formula T_eq = 278.3 · L^0.25 / √a
(L = 0.264) is 326 K at zero albedo, only modestly above Earth's — a
genuinely **temperate** world.

**Scenario choice for NearStars: a temperate mini-Neptune with a thick
volatile envelope, cool hazy cloud bands rendered in muted warm tones
under K3 V orange illumination, kept-but-flagged as observationally
contested, with an unmeasured radius (estimate-only, low confidence).**
At Msini ≈ 10.8 M⊕ and 326 K, g is in the temperate mini-Neptune
regime — too massive to be a bare rock, cool enough that any water
condenses. The radius is unconstrained (RV-only). No documented
divergence — every value traces to the Vogt 2015 Phase 2 recommended
mass, a shown derivation, or an interesting-first tie-break. The
controversy is recorded explicitly rather than as a divergence.

## Surface synthesis

HD 219134 g has no solid surface in the conventional sense — at Msini
≈ 10.8 M⊕ with no measured radius, it is most plausibly a temperate
mini-Neptune with a thick volatile envelope over a rocky-icy core. The
"surface" relevant for visual rendering is the cloud deck, referenced
to the 1 bar pressure level.

The defining caveats are the same as the other RV-only planets, plus
the controversy. First, **g does not transit**: its radius is
unmeasured, so radius, gravity, density, and scale height are estimates
flagged at low confidence. The cfg adopts a sub-Neptune mass-radius
scaling (Lopez & Fortney 2014; Wolfgang 2016): for ~11 M⊕ with a
volatile envelope, R ≈ 2.0–3.0 R⊕, and the cfg picks 2.4 R⊕ as the
mid-range, giving ~1.9 g⊕ surface gravity and ~4.3 g/cc bulk density
(consistent with a volatile-rich mini-Neptune, less dense than the
warmer inner planets because of more condensed volatiles).

Second, **g is observationally contested** (`pl_controv_flag = 1`):
it is one of the two planets added by Vogt 2015 that subsequent work
has questioned. NearStars keeps g for gameplay variety (documented here
and in Open items), with the contested status carried explicitly.

**Color under K3 V illumination.** Because g is the coolest inner
planet (326 K), its condensation clouds are paler and whiter than the
warm tan decks of the hotter d and f. The cfg renders a pale warm-white
cloud deck (`#e8e0d0`) with a muted warm-grey haze limb (`#c8b8a0`) —
tie-break choices, no spectrum exists, defaulting to the temperate
mini-Neptune analog under the 4858 K orange SED.

## Atmosphere synthesis

The atmospheric inference for g is entirely theoretical (no transit, no
spectrum), but the mass and temperature place it confidently in the
temperate mini-Neptune regime.

**Atmosphere present.** At Msini ≈ 11 M⊕ and T_eq ≈ 326 K, g is too
massive to be a bare rock and cool enough that volatiles condense; the
photoevaporation valley (Owen & Wu 2017) leaves a planet of this mass
at 0.38 AU around a K dwarf with its envelope intact. The cfg adopts a
thick H₂/He-dominated atmosphere with enhanced metallicity and a
water/ammonia condensation cloud deck — the canonical temperate
mini-Neptune composition. This is a tie-break (no measured spectrum).

**Sky appearance.** With a thick atmosphere, the sky from the reference
1 bar cloud deck is a pale warm-white overcast, only modestly
brightened toward the substellar side because the insolation is low
(1.87 S⊕, near Earth's). The host star HD 219134 subtends ~1.03°
(about 2× the Sun's apparent size from Earth) and reads warm-orange
through the haze. The atmosphere is cooler and less hazy than the warm
inner planets, so the cloud deck is whiter and the banding crisper.

## Rotation & spin synthesis

At 0.375 AU and 94.2 d, g is far enough out that tidal locking is
firmly excluded — the despinning timescale for a ~11 M⊕ volatile
planet at this separation far exceeds the system age. The cfg adopts a
**freely rotating, non-locked** state.

**KSP implementation note.** With no rotation measurement, the cfg
assigns a generic mini-Neptune rotation period (a moderate ~0.5–1 day
analog is a reasonable placeholder, low confidence). Obliquity is set
to a modest 10° as an interesting-first tie-break — enough to give the
temperate mini-Neptune mild seasonal banding rather than a static
striped disk.

**Mild seasons.** The curated eccentricity is 0.0 (circularized), so
there is no eccentricity-driven seasonal modulation; the 10° obliquity
tie-break provides a gentle latitudinal seasonal cycle over the 94.2 d
orbit, expressed as slow drift in the cloud-band pattern rather than
dramatic weather.

## Visual styling

Combining the atmosphere decisions for a temperate, contested RV-only
mini-Neptune:

- **Global appearance (orbit view).** A pale warm-white banded
  mini-Neptune disk — cooler, whiter, and crisper-banded than the warm
  tan d and f, with subtle latitudinal seasonal structure from the
  10° obliquity.
- **Cloud bands.** Pale warm-white zonal bands (`#e8e0d0`) with a
  water/ammonia condensation cloud deck; banding more distinct than the
  hazier warm inner planets (tie-break — no GCM).
- **Limb haze.** A muted warm-grey haze layer (`#c8b8a0`), thinner and
  less saturated than the hot planets' tan haze, giving a soft
  terminator.
- **Star in sky.** HD 219134 subtends ~1.03° at g — about 2× the Sun's
  angular size from Earth, warm-orange through the haze, illuminating
  the cloud deck at ~1.87× Earth-noon brightness (near Earth-like).
- **Sister planets in sky.** The hot transiting pair (b, c), d, and the
  contested f appear interior; the cold giant h is exterior at ~3 AU.
  All near-coplanar, appearing as bright points.
- **Contested-status rendering note.** g is kept for gameplay variety
  but flagged contested; the rendered planet should be treated as
  provisional, with both its radius (unmeasured) and its existence
  pending observational confirmation.

## Bibliography

### Read (visual-informative, drove decisions above)

- **Vogt S. S. et al. 2015** — *Six Planets Orbiting HD 219134*, ApJ
  814, 12 (`2015ApJ...814...12V`, doi:10.1088/0004-637X/814/1/12).
  Keck/HIRES + APT solution adding g (and f); the source for g's orbit
  (P = 94.2 d, a = 0.3753 AU) and minimum mass (Msini = 10.8 M⊕,
  recommended), and the `pl_controv_flag = 1` controversy flag.
  **The anchor for g's orbit, mass, and contested status.**
- **Lopez E. D. & Fortney J. J. 2014** — *Understanding the
  mass-radius relation for sub-Neptunes*, ApJ 792, 1
  (`2014ApJ...792....1L`, arXiv:1311.0329). Sub-Neptune envelope
  mass-radius scaling; gives the R ≈ 2.0–3.0 R⊕ estimate for ~11 M⊕.
  Drives the (low-confidence) `radius_rearth` estimate.
- **Owen J. E. & Wu Y. 2017** — *The evaporation valley in the Kepler
  planets*, ApJ 847, 29 (`2017ApJ...847...29O`, arXiv:1705.10810).
  Photoevaporation physics; at ~11 M⊕ and 0.38 AU around a K dwarf, g
  retains its volatile envelope. Supports `atmosphere_present = true`.

- **Gillon M. et al. 2017** — Nat. Astron. 1, 0056
  (`2017NatAs...1E..56G`). The transiting b/c study; g is non-

### Not read — no arXiv preprint or low-priority (~handful)

The g-specific arXiv record is small (RV-only, contested, no transit
or atmospheric characterization). The not-read set is RV-reanalysis and
dynamical-stability follow-ups that bear on whether g is real but do
not change the temperate mini-Neptune rendering adopted while the
planet is kept.

## Open items for follow-up

- **Confirmation or retraction.** g carries `pl_controv_flag = 1`; a
  future RV reanalysis or transit detection would confirm or retract
  it. If retracted, g should be removed; if confirmed, the flag clears.
  Currently kept for gameplay variety.
- **True mass and radius.** g's mass is Msini-only and the radius is a
  sub-Neptune scaling estimate at low confidence. An inclination
  (astrometry) or transit detection would pin both.
- **Atmospheric spectrum.** No spectrum exists; a future measurement
  would replace the tie-break composition and cloud tints.
