# GJ 896 A — Phase 3 Synthesis

GJ 896 A (EQ Peg A; BD+19 5116 A; Gaia DR3 2824770686019003904) is the
more massive component of the nearby low-mass M-dwarf binary GJ 896 AB,
at 6.263 pc (Gaia DR3 parallax 159.66 mas) — about 20.4 light-years
from the Sun. The fundamental parameters come from the frozen Phase 2
layer, anchored on Curiel et al. 2022 (VLBA + optical/IR astrometry):
mass M = 0.43599 ± 0.00092 M☉ from the full combined binary-orbit fit,
and a radius R ≈ 0.35 R☉ quoted by Curiel from prior literature (Morin
et al. 2008; Pearce et al. 2020) rather than measured in that work.
The spectral type is M3.5 Ve. Notably, **no effective temperature or
luminosity was curated in Phase 2** — the Curiel astrometric study does
not measure either, so the `teff_measurements` and
`luminosity_measurements` arrays are empty and the cfg Teff/luminosity
are synthesized at low confidence from the spectral type (see below).

What characterizes GJ 896 A for NearStars is that it is a **young, very
active, fast-rotating M3.5 Ve flare star** — one half of the classic
flare-star binary EQ Peg. It rotates extremely fast, with
P_rot = 1.061 d (Curiel 2022, compiling Delfosse 1999 / Morin 2008 /
Davison 2015 / Pearce 2020; the underlying determination is the Morin
et al. 2008 spectropolarimetric/ZDI study), and a rotational
inclination of ~60°. Both members of EQ Peg are long-known radio-burst
sources and flaring X-ray emitters (Pallavicini 1985; Benz 1995;
Crosley & Osten 2018; Villadsen & Hallinan 2019; Robrade 2004; Liefke
2008, all via Curiel 2022). The system's age is debated: Parsamyan 1995
gives ~950 Myr, but several kinematic studies (Riedel 2011; Zuckerman
2013, via Curiel 2022) argue for ≲100 Myr, which would make GJ 896 AB
among the closest pre-main-sequence stars known. The fast rotation and
strong activity are consistent with youth at the saturated end of the
rotation-activity relation.

**Scenario choice for NearStars: a deep-red, young, fast-rotating
(1.061 d) M3.5 Ve flare star with strong starspots, frequent flares,
and a kG-scale magnetic field — the brighter "first sun" of a wide
M-dwarf binary, with a warm Jovian companion on a 284-day orbit.** The
stellar layer is anchored on the frozen Phase 2 source (Curiel 2022
mass, radius, rotation, spectral type). Because Phase 2 carries no Teff
or luminosity measurement, the visual surface tint and color
temperature are synthesized from the M3.5 V spectral type — a deep-red
photosphere in the ~3200–3400 K range — and explicitly flagged as
low-confidence rather than pinned to a precise Teff. No circumstellar
disk is known and none is fabricated. The activity rows (rotation,
flares, magnetic field) are the headline, and the spin/visual choices
sit at medium-to-low confidence.

## Surface synthesis

GJ 896 A has the deep red photosphere typical of an early-mid M dwarf.
There is a structural caveat worth stating plainly: the Phase 2 layer
carries **no measured effective temperature or luminosity** — the
Curiel et al. 2022 astrometric study measures the mass and orbit but
not the photospheric parameters, so its `teff_measurements` and
`luminosity_measurements` arrays are empty. For the cfg the temperature
is therefore synthesized from the M3.5 V spectral type, which
corresponds to a typical Teff in the ~3200–3400 K range (the Gaia DR3
raw catalog value of 3334 K is consistent and is carried only as
context, not as a curated measurement). At this temperature the SED
peaks in the near-infrared and the optical continuum is suppressed by
TiO and VO molecular bands — though less totally than the cooler M4.5
YZ Cet (3100 K) or M7 V Teegarden's Star (2904 K). The rendered color
is correspondingly a deep red-orange, but is explicitly flagged as a
low-confidence synthesis because no Teff was measured.

With R ≈ 0.35 R☉ (Curiel 2022, quoting Morin 2008 / Pearce 2020) and a
synthesized Teff ~3300 K, the Stefan-Boltzmann luminosity is roughly
L ≈ 0.014 L☉; Curiel's own habitable-zone calculation uses a
mass-luminosity relation that implies a similar ~0.02 L☉ for the
0.436 M☉ star. Either way GJ 896 A is roughly 1/50–1/70 of the Sun's
luminosity — low enough that at V = 10.29 it is below naked-eye
visibility despite its 20.4-light-year proximity. These luminosity
figures are used only to bracket the planet's insolation downstream;
they are not curated Phase 2 values.

The defining surface property is strong magnetic activity expressed as
a fast-rotating, heavily-spotted, flaring disk. GJ 896 A is one half of
the well-known flare-star binary EQ Peg, and both components are
long-established radio-burst sources and flaring X-ray emitters
(Pallavicini 1985; Benz 1995; Robrade 2004; Liefke 2008; Crosley &
Osten 2018; Villadsen & Hallinan 2019, all via Curiel 2022). The
extremely fast 1.061-day rotation (rooted in the Morin et al. 2008 ZDI
study) places GJ 896 A in the saturated regime of the
rotation-activity relation, where mid-M dwarfs carry strong, kG-scale,
large-scale magnetic fields. For cfg rendering this means a deep-red
disk carrying a substantial cool-spot fraction that drifts rapidly on
the ~1-day rotation, punctuated by frequent bright flares — a far
livelier, younger-looking surface than the quiet old dwarfs (Barnard,
Teegarden) and more active even than the moderately-active YZ Cet.

No metallicity is curated in the Phase 2 layer and none is synthesized
here; at fixed Teff the metallicity effect on the rendered M-dwarf
color is sub-perceptible, and project policy skips it for new hosts.
There is no debris disk: no infrared-excess detection or resolved
imaging of a disk around GJ 896 A has been reported, so the star is
rendered diskless.

## Atmosphere synthesis

A main-sequence star has no "atmosphere" in the planetary cfg sense;
for a stellar synthesis this section covers the chromosphere, corona,
and — the headline for GJ 896 A — its strong flare and radio-burst
activity.

GJ 896 A's chromosphere and corona are those of a young, very active
mid-M dwarf at the saturated end of the activity sequence. The Phase 2
layer carries no quantitative chromospheric (log R'HK) or coronal
(log L_X) index — the Curiel 2022 astrometric study does not measure
them — so the activity is characterized qualitatively here from the
EQ Peg literature that Curiel compiles. Both EQ Peg components are
flaring X-ray emitters (Robrade 2004; Liefke 2008) and recurrent
radio-burst sources (Pallavicini 1985; Benz 1995; Crosley & Osten 2018;
Villadsen & Hallinan 2019). The extremely fast 1.061-day rotation
implies a very small Rossby number and hence a saturated dynamo, the
regime where mid-M dwarfs reach their maximum fractional X-ray and
chromospheric output and carry the strongest large-scale fields. The
Morin et al. 2008 ZDI study of EQ Peg A (the source of the rotation
period) maps exactly this kind of strong, ordered kG field.

The headline feature is flaring. GJ 896 A is a textbook UV-Cet-class
flare star: it produces frequent optical flares and was detected at
multiple VLBA epochs with compact, variable radio emission (Bower 2009,
2011, via Curiel 2022) — the radio variability is itself the signature
of an active corona and recurrent flaring. For cfg purposes the corona
/ chromosphere layer is "very active with frequent flares," distinct
from the quiescent-with-rare-flares old dwarfs (Barnard, Teegarden) and
more active than the moderately-active YZ Cet. The fast rotation also
means the spotted disk and any flare-related brightening modulate on a
roughly daily cadence rather than the months-long periods of the slow
rotators.

## Rotation & spin synthesis

GJ 896 A rotates very fast, with P_rot = 1.061 d (Curiel 2022,
compiling Delfosse 1999 / Morin 2008 / Davison 2015 / Pearce 2020; the
underlying determination is the Morin et al. 2008 spectropolarimetric /
ZDI study of EQ Peg A). This is among the fastest rotators in the
NearStars M-dwarf set — roughly 65× faster than the moderately-active
YZ Cet (68.4 d) and ~90× faster than the old quiet Teegarden's Star
(96 d). Such rapid rotation is the fingerprint of youth: the star has
not yet spun down through magnetic braking, consistent with the
≲100 Myr pre-main-sequence age that Riedel 2011 and Zuckerman 2013
argue for (Curiel 2022 also notes the older ~950 Myr Parsamyan 1995
estimate, so the age itself is uncertain and not Phase-2-curated). The
fast spin drives the saturated dynamo behind the strong activity.

**KSP implementation note.** Stellar rotation period = 1.061 d =
91 670 s. In Kopernicus the star body's `rotationPeriod` is set in
seconds; at ~1 day the rotation is fast enough that rotational
modulation of the spotted disk is visible on a roughly daily cadence,
unlike the effectively-static slow rotators elsewhere in the catalog.

The rotational inclination is ~60° (Curiel 2022, from the same ZDI
literature compilation). For NearStars visual rendering this is taken
as the spin-axis orientation, giving an intermediate-tilt view of the
spotted disk rather than pole-on or perfectly equator-on; it informs
which hemisphere's starspots and flare footpoints are most visible but
is not otherwise cfg-decisive. No asteroseismic constraint is available
(the star is far too cool and too small for detectable p-mode
oscillations).

## Visual styling

In the NearStars renderer, GJ 896 A is portrayed as a pale warm orange,
young, fast-rotating M3.5 Ve flare star — the brighter "first sun" of a
wide M-dwarf binary:

- **Global appearance.** A pale warm orange disk encoded as `#ffd088`,
  the real integrated-SED color close to the ~3300 K blackbody; the
  TiO/VO bands shift the displayed chromaticity only modestly. The
  M-sequence chromaticity is nearly flat, so this sits essentially
  alongside the M4 V Barnard (`#ffd487`) and M4.5 YZ Cet (`#ffd081`),
  all within the pale-warm-orange family. Because no Teff is measured,
  this hue is a low-confidence synthesis from the M3.5 V spectral type
  rather than a Teff-pinned value. The scene-illumination color
  temperature for the planet is driven by the synthesized ~3300 K SED.
- **Heavily spotted surface.** A substantial cool-spot fraction
  (`visual_spot_coverage_max ≈ 0.2`) drifting rapidly on the 1.061-day
  rotation — strong rotational brightness modulation befitting a young,
  saturated-dynamo flare star, far more active than the near-uniform
  disks of the old quiet dwarfs.
- **Frequent flares.** Bright optical flares (UV-Cet eruptive variable,
  the EQ Peg signature) rendered as recurrent transient brightenings on
  a roughly daily cadence — the liveliest stellar surface among the
  catalog's M dwarfs, short of a continuously-erupting protostar.
- **A second red sun (binary companion).** GJ 896 A shares the system
  with GJ 896 B, an M4.5 dwarf (0.16527 M☉, R ≈ 0.25 R☉, P_rot 0.404 d)
  on a wide a = 31.635 AU, 229-year **retrograde** orbit (mutual
  inclination Φ = 148° between the binary and planetary planes; Curiel
  2022). From GJ 896 A's planet b, GJ 896 B is a second deep-red sun —
  far smaller than the primary in the sky but a distinct bright point,
  itself an active flare star. (GJ 896 B is not yet a separate DB body;
  flagged for follow-up.)
- **Star in planet b's sky.** From planet b at the semi-major axis
  a = 0.64 AU, GJ 896 A subtends ~0.29° — a little over half the Sun's
  angular size from Earth; near perihelion (0.42 AU) it grows to
  ~0.44°, close to the Sun's apparent size, and the deep-red disk
  floods the warm Jovian with infrared-rich light at a few percent of
  Earth's insolation.
- **From Earth.** GJ 896 A is a V = 10.29 telescopic object, below
  naked-eye visibility despite its 20.4-light-year proximity, because
  of its low M-dwarf luminosity.

## Open items for follow-up

- **GJ 896 B as a separate DB body.** The M4.5 companion (0.16527 M☉,
  R ≈ 0.25 R☉, P_rot 0.404 d, a = 31.635 AU, P = 229.06 yr, retrograde)
  is a second active flare star and a "second sun" in the planet's sky.
  A follow-up should add GJ 896 B to the DB and produce its own stellar
  Phase 3 synthesis, plus the binary_orbit cfg for the wide retrograde
  pair.
- **Teff / luminosity measurement.** No Phase 2 Teff or luminosity is
  curated; the cfg synthesizes them from the M3.5 V spectral type at
  low confidence. A future curation of a measured Teff (e.g. from a
  CARMENES / SED study of EQ Peg A) should replace the synthesized
  ~3300 K and sharpen the surface-tint hex from a low-confidence to a
  high-confidence value.
- **Age resolution.** The ~950 Myr vs ≲100 Myr (PMS) age ambiguity is
  unresolved. If confirmed pre-main-sequence, GJ 896 AB would be among
  the closest PMS stars known, which would justify an even
  more-active, possibly inflated-radius rendering.
- **Quantitative activity indices.** No log R'HK, X-ray flux, or flare
  rate is curated. A dedicated EQ Peg A activity census (Ca II H&K,
  Chandra/XMM, TESS flares) would let a future cfg attach quantitative
  `activity_log_rhk`, `x_ray_log_lx`, and `flare_rate_per_day` values to
  the currently-qualitative activity rendering.
- **Magnetic-field map.** The Morin 2008 ZDI provides a kG-scale field
  qualitatively; a refreshed large-scale field map would pin the dipole
  geometry for a faithful spot / flare-footpoint animation keyed to the
  1.061-day rotation.
