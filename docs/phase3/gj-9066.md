# GJ 9066 — Phase 3 Synthesis

GJ 9066 (= TZ Arietis = GJ 83.1 = LHS 11 = Karmn J02002+130; Gaia DR3
76868614540049408) is an M4.5 V red dwarf at 4.470 pc — about 14.6
light-years away, a single star with no known stellar companion. It is
one of the closest red dwarfs to the Sun, but at V ≈ 12.4 (Gaia G =
10.68) it is far below naked-eye visibility: the optical continuum is
heavily suppressed by molecular bands and the bulk of the flux emerges
in the near-infrared. The fundamental parameters come from the frozen
Phase 2 layer, drawn from the CARMENES survey: effective temperature
Teff = 3100 K (Cifuentes et al. 2020, SED fitting; the independent
spectroscopic determinations cluster at 3154 ± 54 K, Passegger 2019,
and 3193 ± 51 K, Schweitzer 2019), luminosity L = 0.00254 ± 0.00002 L☉
(Schweitzer et al. 2019, bolometric flux), radius R = 0.1646 ± 0.0053 R☉
and mass M = 0.1478 ± 0.0036 M☉ (both Schweitzer et al. 2019). At only
~1/394 of the Sun's luminosity it is among the dimmest hosts in the
catalog, deep-red in tint.

What characterizes GJ 9066 for NearStars is that it is a **very active
fast-rotating flare star** — the opposite of the old quiet dwarfs
Barnard and Teegarden. It rotates in only P_rot = 1.96 ± 0.02 d
(Quirrenbach et al. 2022, OSN R + V photometry plus chromatic RV and
Hα / Ca II IRT line modulation), one of the fastest-rotating hosts in
the set, and it carries the historical variable-star designation
TZ Arietis precisely because it flares: it is a BY-Draconis spotted
rotator and a UV-Ceti eruptive flare star, with strong persistent Hα
emission (pEW′ = −1.906 ± 0.025 Å, Schöfer et al. 2019) and white-light
flares reaching ΔV ≈ 0.87 mag in the ASAS-SN light curve (Rodríguez
Martínez et al. 2020). The combination — fast rotation, strong dynamo,
frequent red flaring skies — defines its visual personality.

GJ 9066 hosts a single confirmed planet, **GJ 9066 c**: a warm,
strongly eccentric (e = 0.46) sub-Saturn-mass giant at a = 0.88 AU
(M sin i ≈ 0.21 M_Jup ≈ 67 M⊕; Quirrenbach et al. 2022). It is one of
only a handful of giant planets known around a host below 0.3 M☉. A
second Feng et al. 2020 candidate ("GJ 9066 b", P ≈ 242 d) was
**refuted** by Quirrenbach 2022 as a rotation/activity artifact and is
not carried as a planet. The planet is treated in its own Phase 3
synthesis (`gj-9066-c.md`).

**Scenario choice for NearStars: a deep-red, very active,
fast-rotating M4.5 V flare star — a BY-Draconis / UV-Ceti eruptive
variable with strong starspots and frequent white-light flares —
anchoring a single warm, highly eccentric sub-Saturn giant.** The
stellar layer is anchored on the frozen Phase 2 sources (Cifuentes
2020 Teff, Schweitzer 2019 luminosity/radius/mass, Quirrenbach 2022
rotation/activity). No metallicity or age array is curated; no
circumstellar disk is known and none is fabricated. One tie-break sets
the visual surface tint for the 3100 K M4.5 V SED; the spot-coverage
and flare-rate rows sit at low/medium confidence because no quantitative
filling factor or flare-frequency distribution is in the Phase 2 layer.

## Surface synthesis

GJ 9066 has the deep red photosphere typical of a mid-M dwarf. At
Teff = 3100 K (Cifuentes 2020) the SED peaks in the near-infrared near
~0.93 μm, and the optical continuum is heavily suppressed by TiO and
VO molecular bands below ~0.7 μm — though not as totally strangled as
the cooler M7 V Teegarden's Star (2904 K). The spectroscopic Teff
determinations run a little warmer than the SED value — Passegger 2019
gives 3154 ± 54 K and Schweitzer 2019 gives 3193 ± 51 K — but the ~90 K
spread is sub-perceptible in the rendered tint. With R = 0.1646 R☉
(Schweitzer 2019) and L = 0.00254 L☉ the star is roughly 1/394 of the
Sun's luminosity, far too faint to see by eye (V ≈ 12.4) despite its
14.6-light-year proximity.

Unlike the directly-resolved CHARA radius of Barnard's Star, the
GJ 9066 radius is an SED quantity: at R = 0.1646 R☉ and 4.470 pc the
limb-darkened angular diameter is only ~0.34 mas, below the ground-based
interferometric floor, so Schweitzer 2019 closed Stefan-Boltzmann from
the bolometric luminosity and Teff rather than measuring the disk. No
metallicity is curated in the Phase 2 layer and none is synthesized
here; the literature [Fe/H] values scatter (Passegger 2019 −0.13,
Marfil 2021 −0.25) and at fixed Teff the metallicity effect on the
rendered M-dwarf color is sub-perceptible, so project policy skips it.

The defining surface property is **strong magnetic activity expressed
as a fast-rotating, spotted, flaring disk** — the opposite extreme from
the catalog's old quiet dwarfs. The fast 1.96-day rotation (Quirrenbach
2022) drives a vigorous dynamo: the chromospheric Ca II H&K index sits
in the active regime (log R'HK ≈ −4.79 to −4.16 across analyses) and the
Hα is strongly and persistently in emission (pEW′ = −1.906 Å; Schöfer
2019). Interestingly the R-band photometric rotational amplitude is
small (~3 mmag; Quirrenbach 2022), yet the chromatic RV modulation at
1.96 d is strong, which Quirrenbach interprets as chromospheric activity
distributed fairly uniformly in longitude over a "significantly
structured photosphere." For cfg rendering this means a deep-red disk
carrying a moderate cool-spot fraction drifting on the fast 1.96-day
rotation, punctuated by frequent bright white-light flares — a much
livelier surface than Barnard or Teegarden. There is no debris disk:
no infrared-excess detection or resolved imaging has been reported, so
the star is rendered diskless.

## Atmosphere synthesis

A main-sequence star has no "atmosphere" in the planetary cfg sense;
for a stellar synthesis this section covers the chromosphere, corona,
and flares — and for GJ 9066 the activity is the headline.

GJ 9066's chromosphere and corona are those of an active mid-M dwarf
driven by fast rotation. The chromospheric Ca II H&K index is in the
active regime — Astudillo-Defru 2017 gives log R'HK = −4.79 ± 0.42,
Perger 2021 −4.46, Boro Saikia 2018 −4.16 (all via Quirrenbach 2022
Table 1) — and the Hα line is strongly in emission with temporally
variable equivalent width (pEW′ = −1.906 Å; Schöfer 2019), the marker
of a persistently warm, active chromosphere. The 1.96-day rotation gives
a very small Rossby number, placing GJ 9066 near or in the saturated
regime of the rotation–activity relation, where fast rotation begets a
strong dynamo begets an active corona and chromosphere. This is the
dynamical opposite of Barnard's 145-day-rotation floor-level quiescence.

The defining feature is **frequent flaring**. GJ 9066 is a UV-Ceti
eruptive flare star (the historical reason for its TZ Arietis
variable-star designation; Kunkel 1968), and the ASAS-SN monitoring
caught white-light flares with amplitude up to ΔV ≈ 0.87 mag (Rodríguez
Martínez et al. 2020) — large optical brightenings against the deep-red
quiescent continuum. Combined with the persistent Hα emission and the
active corona, the picture is of a star that flares often and brightly
rather than the rare-event punctuation of the old quiet dwarfs. For cfg
purposes the corona/chromosphere layer is "very active with frequent
flares," and the flares are a near-continuous visual feature, not a rare
event. No quantitative flare-frequency distribution is in the frozen
Phase 2 layer, so the flare rate is left qualitative (flagged in Open
items).

The activity has a direct bearing on the planet: GJ 9066 c orbits at
a = 0.88 AU, far outside any habitable zone but bathed in the
time-averaged XUV and frequent flare output of an active M dwarf. Over
the star's lifetime the cumulative high-energy dose at the planet is far
higher than around a quiet dwarf — relevant context for any atmospheric
chemistry on the giant or its hypothetical moons.

## Rotation & spin synthesis

GJ 9066 rotates fast, with a photometric period P_rot = 1.96 ± 0.02 d
(Quirrenbach 2022). The period is robustly triple-anchored: the OSN R
and V photometry both show a strong 1.96 d peak (with its 2.03 d daily
alias), the CARMENES RVs show a strong chromatic 1.96 d modulation that
cannot be a Doppler companion signal, and the Hα and Ca II IRT activity
indicators vary at the same period. This supersedes the older ~30 d
rotation value (Astudillo-Defru 2017, cited by Feng 2020), which the
multi-indicator CARMENES analysis revises decisively. A 1.96-day period
is among the fastest in the M-dwarf catalog — the dynamical opposite of
the very old slow rotators Barnard (145 d) and Teegarden (96 d).

This fast rotation is the engine of the star's activity: at such a small
Rossby number the rotation–activity relation predicts exactly the
active-regime chromospheric and coronal emission observed. The young-ish
age implied (no younger than the Hyades, ~750 Myr; Quirrenbach 2022) is
consistent with a star that has not yet had time to magnetically brake
to slow rotation. There is no measured activity cycle.

**KSP implementation note.** Stellar rotation period = 1.96 d =
169 344 s. In Kopernicus the star body's `rotationPeriod` is set in
seconds; unlike the slow old dwarfs, this fast rotation is short enough
that a NearStars time-lapse could show visible rotation of the spotted
disk over in-game hours.

The projected rotational velocity is only weakly constrained: Reiners
2018 sets v sin i < 2 km/s (via Quirrenbach 2022), while the fast 1.96 d
rotation at R = 0.1646 R☉ implies an equatorial velocity of ~4.3 km/s.
The two are reconciled either by a low rotation-axis inclination
(sin i small, the system seen relatively pole-on) or by the < 2 km/s
being a conservative upper limit at CARMENES resolution; the cfg records
v sin i as an upper limit rather than a measurement. No asteroseismic
constraint is available — GJ 9066 is far too cool and small for
detectable p-mode oscillations. The rotation-axis inclination is
otherwise unconstrained; for visual rendering NearStars adopts a generic
axis orientation, though here — unlike the near-stationary old dwarfs —
the fast spin does make the spotted-disk rotation a visible animated
feature.

## Visual styling

In the NearStars renderer, GJ 9066 is portrayed as a deep red-orange
M4.5 V flare star whose signature is its activity rather than its modest
photosphere:

- **Global appearance.** A deep red-orange disk encoded as `#cf5630`,
  the 3100 K blackbody continuum after TiO/VO band suppression of the
  blue — essentially the same hue as the M4.5 V YZ Cet (`#cf5630`,
  identical Teff) and the M4 V Barnard's Star (`#cf5a30`), and redder
  than the warmer K/M hosts in the catalog. The illumination color
  temperature for scene lighting of the planet is driven directly by the
  3100 K SED.
- **Spotted surface.** A moderate cool-spot fraction
  (`visual_spot_coverage_max ≈ 0.15`) drifting on the fast 1.96-day
  rotation. The photometric amplitude is small, but the strong RV
  modulation implies a structured photosphere — rendered as
  slowly-evolving dark spot groups that rotate visibly across the disk on
  the short period.
- **Flares (the headline).** Frequent bright white-light flares — a
  UV-Ceti eruptive variable with flares reaching ΔV ≈ 0.87 mag (Rodríguez
  Martínez 2020). Rendered as recurrent, prominent transient brightenings
  of the deep-red disk: this is a genuinely flaring star, the active
  opposite of the quiescent old dwarfs Barnard and Teegarden, and the
  flaring should read as a near-continuous feature.
- **Nightside / corona.** A persistently warm chromosphere and active
  corona (strong Hα emission) support a faint chromospheric/coronal glow
  at the limb, brightening during flare events.
- **Star in the planet's sky.** From GJ 9066 c (a = 0.88 AU) the star
  subtends only ~0.10° — about 1/5 the Sun's angular size from Earth —
  swinging between ~0.18° at periastron (0.475 AU) and ~0.068° at
  apoastron (1.285 AU) over the eccentric orbit. The disk is a small,
  deep red-orange, frequently-flaring point that floods the giant in
  faint infrared-rich light.
- **From Earth.** GJ 9066 is a V ≈ 12.4 telescopic object, invisible to
  the naked eye despite its 14.6-light-year proximity, because of the
  tiny 0.00254 L☉ luminosity.

## Open items for follow-up

- **Planet Phase 3 synthesis.** The single confirmed planet GJ 9066 c
  (warm, e = 0.46, M sin i ≈ 0.21 M_Jup sub-Saturn at 0.88 AU) is
  produced as a separate Phase 3 doc (`gj-9066-c.md`). The refuted Feng
  2020 "GJ 9066 b" (242 d) should be recorded as refuted, mirroring the
  tau Cet e / 40 Eri A b treatment.
- **Flare-rate quantification.** The UV-Ceti classification and the
  ASAS-SN ΔV ≈ 0.87 flare are qualitative; TZ Ari was not observed by
  TESS in cycles 1–4 (Quirrenbach 2022). A dedicated TESS / ground flare
  census would let a future cfg attach a quantitative `flare_rate_per_day`
  and `flare_energy_log_erg_max` to the rendering.
- **Spot-coverage filling factor.** No measured cool-spot filling factor
  exists; the `visual_spot_coverage_max ≈ 0.15` is a tie-break. A
  Zeeman-Doppler-Imaging map or a TESS amplitude census would replace it.
- **Rotation-axis inclination / v sin i tension.** The Reiners 2018
  v sin i < 2 km/s upper limit sits below the ~4.3 km/s equatorial
  velocity implied by the 1.96 d rotation and 0.1646 R☉ radius, hinting
  at a low rotation-axis inclination. A measured inclination would both
  resolve this and (via the RV M sin i) bound the planet's true mass.
- **Age.** No Phase 2 age array. If a future curation adds a
  gyrochronology or kinematic age, the low-confidence "> ~750 Myr"
  context bound should be replaced.
