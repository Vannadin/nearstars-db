# HD 69830 d — Phase 3 Synthesis

HD 69830 d is the outermost of three Neptune-mass radial-velocity
planets discovered by Lovis et al. 2006 (HARPS, 4-year campaign):
P = 197 ± 3 d, Msini = 18.1 M⊕ (Lovis 2006),
a = 0.63 AU, e = 0.07, ω = 224°. With the host star at
L = 0.60 L☉ this places d at ~1.5× Earth's insolation and an
equilibrium temperature of 282 K (A = 0.3 Bond albedo) — within
the optimistic habitable zone of HD 69830 but with a Neptune-mass
envelope rather than a rocky surface. The orbit at 0.63 AU sits
just interior to the warm dust population's inner edge at 0.7–0.8
AU (Beichman 2005), placing d at the dust belt's inner shoulder
in the position dynamically privileged for shepherding the inner
edge and serving as a plausible parent body source for the
catastrophic-event-class debris hypothesised by Lisse 2007. No
transit detection: Bonfils 2007 and follow-up photometry exclude
the predicted ephemeris.

**Scenario choice for NearStars: a temperate envelope-retaining
Neptune analog at the dust belt's inner shoulder — full
Neptune-class H₂/He atmosphere, water-rich icy interior, and a
near-pristine deep-cyan Neptune palette modulated by the cooler
G8V illumination. The planet's geometric proximity to the warm
dust belt makes it the dynamical centerpiece of the system: d's
gravity is strong enough at 18 M⊕ to perturb a population of
parent bodies in the 0.7–1.5 AU region, and Lisse 2007's
catastrophic-event hypothesis identifies d as the plausible
sculptor of the belt's inner edge.** 28 cfg picks; 14
canonical-aligned (orbital + mass parameters, Neptune-analog
radius / density, derived insolation), 14 tie-break (deep-cyan
Neptune tint, cloud banding morphology, dust-shepherding role).
No documented divergences — d sits comfortably in the
envelope-retention locus and its visible appearance follows the
Neptune / Uranus literature directly.

## Surface synthesis

HD 69830 d has no geological surface — it is the most canonical
Neptune-analog of the three Lovis 2006 planets. At 18.1 M⊕ and
1.5 S⊕, d sits firmly in the envelope-retention locus (Fulton
2017, Rogers 2021): the photoevaporation timescale at 0.63 AU
around an old G8V is several tens of Gyr, far longer than the
system age, and the host XUV luminosity (log L_X ≈ 26.7) is too
feeble to drive measurable mass-loss from a Neptune-class envelope
at this orbital distance. The cfg adopts the canonical interior
structure: a small (~3 M⊕) rocky/iron core, a deep (~9 M⊕)
supercritical H₂O / NH₃ / CH₄ ice mantle, and a thick (~6 M⊕,
~30% of total mass) H₂/He envelope.

The "surface" the renderer sees is the tropospheric methane-cloud
deck at ~1 bar. This is shallower than c's haze-deck visible
photosphere (c at ~0.1 bar) because d's deeper atmosphere is
cooler and methane condensation is the dominant cloud-formation
mechanism in the upper troposphere. Below the methane cloud,
the atmosphere transitions to a hydrogen-rich Rayleigh-scattering
regime that gives d its defining deep-cyan tint — the same
physics that makes Neptune blue, modulated only slightly by the
host star's marginally cooler illumination.

The conceptual interior color for the cfg-variant cutaway renderer
encodes a Neptune-analog deep mantle: `#3a5060` deep blue-grey
for the supercritical ice mantle dominant body, with `#5a7080`
slightly warmer blue-grey accents at convective plume bases. The
inner rocky/iron core follows the host star metallicity scaling
([Fe/H] = −0.05). The envelope's deep base reaches ~3500 K at the
ice-mantle / envelope boundary (pressure ~80 kbar), with the
ice mantle itself reaching ~6000 K at the rock-core boundary —
all far below visible photon escape depths.

The internal energy budget for d is dominated by primordial heat
of formation plus radiogenic decay, with tidal dissipation
(~0.0001 W/m² from e = 0.07 + Neptune-class Q) contributing
negligibly. Neptune itself emits ~2.6× the radiation it absorbs
from the Sun, implying a substantial intrinsic heat flux (~0.4
W/m²); the cfg assumes d follows the Neptune analog and includes
no special interior heat source beyond the primordial budget. The
modest tidal heating at e = 0.07 is well below the threshold
where it would meaningfully modify the envelope structure.

**Parent body for the warm dust belt? (Lisse 2007 hypothesis).**
Lisse 2007's interpretation of the warm dust at 0.7–1.5 AU is
post-collisional debris from a recent (≲ 1 Myr) catastrophic
disruption of a dwarf-planet-class body. d at 0.63 AU is the
nearest substantial gravitational perturber to this region, and
its 18.1 M⊕ mass is large enough to gravitationally focus
asteroid-class bodies into stable resonant orbits (Lisse 2007
identifies the 2:1 and 5:2 outer mean-motion resonances with d
at 1.00 and 1.16 AU respectively, both squarely within the belt;
the 3:2 outer MMR at 0.83 AU is a secondary near the cfg-widened
belt inner edge). Under the catastrophic-event
hypothesis, d's gravity is the natural sculptor of the belt's
inner edge and the plausible source of the dynamically-excited
parent body whose destruction supplied the observed sub-μm
crystalline olivine grains. This is informational for the cfg's
visual styling: d is rendered as a Neptune-blue disk with no
direct dust feature attached, but the system-overview render in
the host star file (`hd-69830.html`) shows d's geometric
relationship to the belt prominently.

## Atmosphere synthesis

The atmosphere is d's dominant observable, and unlike b's haze-
obscured stripped envelope or c's chromophore-tinted warm haze,
d's atmosphere appears as essentially a smaller Neptune. The
temperate equilibrium temperature 282 K places d firmly in the
"cold Neptune" regime where CH₄ is the dominant carbon carrier
in both the gas and condensed-phase upper troposphere, NH₃ is
condensed out below the methane cloud, and the visible photosphere
is dominated by Rayleigh scattering off the deep H₂ envelope with
methane absorption bands carving the red end of the visible
spectrum. This gives d its characteristic blue color — the same
mechanism that makes Neptune deeper blue than Uranus despite
their nearly identical compositions.

The cfg adopts Karkoschka 2011's Neptune-analog cloud morphology
with broken methane-ice cap clouds covering ~55% of the disk,
predominantly at mid-latitudes where convective updrafts bring
methane-rich air above the condensation altitude. Banded zonal
flow develops with ~5 alternating darker (clearer, deeper) and
brighter (cloudier, higher) bands across the disk, comparable to
Neptune's morphology but slightly enhanced by d's stronger
solar heating. Transient bright methane storms appear at
mid-latitudes on decadal timescales (Sromovsky 2001 Neptune analog),
and occasional Neptune-like Great Dark Spots — large
anticyclonic vortices — develop, persist for years, then
dissipate.

The atmospheric tint `#3a7090` is Neptune's `#2a6ea0` warmed by
~5% toward the green-blue under HD 69830's slightly cooler
G8V illumination. The shift is subtle — the perceived color is
still unambiguously "Neptune blue" — but the cfg encodes the
small chromatic adjustment to differentiate d visually from a
straight Neptune-copy. The cloud tint `#a0d0e0` is the bright
methane-ice cap white-cyan, providing the bright zonal-band
contrast against the deeper Rayleigh-blue Neptune background.

**Sky from d's substellar region.** Above the methane cloud deck,
the star fills a 0.67° disk in the sky — slightly larger than
the Sun seen from Earth (0.53°) — with the slightly redder-than-Sol
cream-yellow tint giving the daytime sky a warm cyan-cream
appearance (Rayleigh-scattered G8V illumination off d's H₂
atmosphere). The warm dust belt at 0.7–1.5 AU is *exterior* to d
by only 10–60% in semi-major axis: from d's vantage the belt
fills a wide swath of the sky as a bright amber band, vastly more
prominent than from b or c. The belt extends nearly to d's own
orbital region, with parent bodies (if Lisse 2007's hypothesis
holds) potentially visible as transient point sources in d's sky
during favourable geometry.

## Rotation & spin synthesis

Tidal damping for d at 197-day orbital period over 5.7 ± 1 Gyr
is utterly negligible: the synchronisation timescale for a
Neptune-class planet at 0.63 AU around an old G-dwarf is ≈ 10¹¹
years (Heller 2011 scaling), far longer than the system age and
far longer than the universe. d retains its primordial spin
period and obliquity. The cfg adopts a Neptune-analog rotation
of ~16 hours and an obliquity of 28° (very close to Neptune's
28.3°), giving the planet a fast-rotating, dynamically active
appearance with seasonal effects from the tilted spin axis.

Differential rotation is expected Neptune-like with the equator
rotating ~10–15% faster than the polar latitudes (Sromovsky 1993,
2001), driving the banded zonal-flow morphology and the transient
spot/storm features. The fast rotation period combined with the
modest 280 K temperature produces a moderately strong zonal-jet
regime — Showman & Polvani 2011 / Polichtchouk 2014 predict
~5 alternating bands across each hemisphere, with the brightest
methane-cloud cap concentrated at the equator and mid-latitudes.

**KSP implementation note.** Rotation period = 16.11 hours
(58 000 s) under the Neptune-analog cfg. Obliquity = 28° tilted
from the orbital plane normal, with the rotation axis pointing
toward an arbitrary direction in inertial space (cfg adopts the
same Neptune-analog longitude of ascending node as the planet's
orbit for simplicity).

**Seasonal cycling at e = 0.07.** The mild eccentricity drives a
~30% insolation amplitude over each 197-day orbit, with
perihelion (0.586 AU) → 1.74 S⊕ and aphelion (0.674 AU) →
1.32 S⊕. Combined with the 28° obliquity, d experiences both
eccentricity-driven and obliquity-driven seasons over the
6.5-month orbital period — the obliquity drives the dominant
hemispheric asymmetry, with the eccentricity providing a modest
global modulation. The seasonal pattern is therefore richer than
Neptune's (which has a 165-year period and 28° obliquity but
near-zero eccentricity) but compressed into a much shorter
orbital cycle. Not animated by the cfg; informational.

## Visual styling

- **Global appearance.** A deep Neptune-cyan disk (`#3a7090`
  primary tint, `#a0d0e0` bright methane-cloud highlights)
  about 0.06° apparent diameter from a viewpoint at HD 69830 c
  during opposition. Banded zonal-flow structure is visible at
  orbit-view scale, with ~5 alternating darker/brighter bands
  per hemisphere. The visual signature is unambiguously
  "Neptune-class" — the most familiar of the three planets in
  the system.
- **Dayside detail.** Modest substellar / equatorial brightening
  from enhanced methane-cloud activity at the warmest latitudes;
  ~5% peak-to-peak contrast over the global tint.
- **Polar caps.** Tilted 28° obliquity gives d distinct polar
  regions visible from different orbital viewpoints. The pole
  facing the star at orbital "summer" shows reduced cloud
  cover (~30%) and a deeper blue Rayleigh-scattering exposure;
  the winter pole shows enhanced methane condensation and a
  brighter white-cyan cap (~75% cloud cover).
- **Transient features.** Occasional bright methane storm cells
  at mid-latitudes (lifespan ~ months) and large anticyclonic
  dark spots ("d-class GDS") at sub-decadal cadence. The cfg
  optionally animates these on a stochastic schedule with
  amplitude ~10% over the local cloud-tint baseline.
- **Terminator band.** Gradient deep cyan to dim blue-grey
  (`#1a3848`) with a Rayleigh-scattering profile that gives
  the day-night limb a soft, extended ~3° feathered edge.
- **Nightside.** Dim blue-grey glow from residual thermal
  emission at 270 K — Neptune's 47 K is too cold to glow
  visibly in the renderer, but d at 270 K shows a faint thermal
  background.
- **Star in sky.** HD 69830 fills 0.67° in d's sky — slightly
  larger than the Sun from Earth, a small bright cream-yellow
  disk. From d's vantage the star appears at typical
  HZ-comparable angular diameter, giving the daytime sky an
  Earth-like brightness level (modulated by d's higher
  atmospheric albedo).
- **Sister planets in sky.** From d, planet b appears at
  ~0.005° during conjunction (a/b separation 0.55 AU, b at
  Sun's glare), and c at ~0.005° during c's closest approach
  (a/c separation 0.44 AU, c's haze-disk radius ~22 000 km).
  Both inner planets are essentially point sources from d.
- **Warm dust ring from d's perspective.** This is d's most
  dramatic sky feature. The dust belt at 0.7–1.5 AU extends
  from just exterior to d's orbit (0.7 AU is 11% beyond d's
  semi-major axis 0.63 AU) outward to 1.5 AU, projecting from
  d's vantage as a bright amber band ringing the sky at the
  forward orbital hemisphere. The belt is several degrees
  thick from d's viewpoint and significantly brighter than from
  any of the inner planets. Forward-scattering off sub-μm
  olivine grains during morning / evening crescent geometry
  produces a striking bright limb feature.

## Open items for follow-up

- **Transit search refresh.** No published transit detection as of
  2026; the 197 d period is challenging for ground-based transit
  surveys but within CHEOPS / PLATO long-baseline capability. A
  transit detection would resolve the mass-radius tie-break and
  update `radius_rearth` from the canonical 4.45 R⊕.
- **Dust-belt resolved imaging.** SPHERE / JWST-MIRI / future ALMA
  imaging of the warm dust belt could constrain d's role as the
