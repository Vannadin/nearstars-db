# HD 219134 b — Phase 3 Synthesis

HD 219134 b is the innermost of six planets around the bright quiet
K3 V dwarf HD 219134, and one of only two that **transit**. Discovered
in RV by Motalebi et al. 2015 and confirmed transiting by Gillon et al.
2017 with Spitzer photometry, it has both a measured true mass and a
measured radius: M = 4.74 ± 0.19 M⊕, R = 1.602 ± 0.055 R⊕, on a
3.0937-day orbit at a = 0.0382 AU. The bulk density follows directly,
ρ ≈ 6.4 g/cc — denser than Earth, placing b among the highest-density
known transiting super-Earths and implying a rocky/iron composition
with no significant volatile envelope. The equilibrium temperature is
high: TEPCat lists T_eq ≈ 1015 K, and the project formula
T_eq = 278.3 · L^0.25 / √a (L = 0.264) gives 1021 K at zero albedo.

**Scenario choice for NearStars: a hot, dense, airless rocky
super-Earth with a dark basaltic-to-partial-melt surface, a cooling-
lava accent near the substellar point, and no visible atmosphere.**
At 1015 K and ~181× Earth's insolation, b sits in the hot-rocky regime
where any primordial H/He envelope was photoevaporated long ago and a
secondary atmosphere is difficult to retain under the close-in XUV
flux; the high measured density independently rules out a thick
volatile envelope. The alternative — a retained thin high-mean-
molecular-weight (CO₂ / silicate-vapor) atmosphere — is preserved as a
cfg variant in Open items, but the density and insolation favor the
airless reading.

## Surface synthesis

HD 219134 b is the densest and hottest of the system's planets, and
the strongest observational anchor in the family because it both
transits and has a precise RV mass. Three anchors pin the synthesis:

1. **Measured mass and radius.** Gillon 2017's Spitzer transit gives
   R = 1.602 ± 0.055 R⊕, and the combined transit + RV solution gives
   M = 4.74 ± 0.19 M⊕. The bulk density ρ ≈ 6.4 g/cc (derived) is
   higher than Earth's 5.51 g/cc — Gillon 2017 emphasize b and c as
   among the densest known transiting super-Earths, consistent with an
   Earth-like-to-iron-rich rocky composition and no significant
   gaseous envelope. This is the decisive constraint: b is rock, not a
   mini-Neptune.

2. **Insolation 181 S⊕, T_eq ≈ 1015 K.** This is the hot-rocky / lava-
   world regime, at the cooler end of the family that Léger 2009
   introduced with CoRoT-7b (T_eq ≈ 1810 K) and that 55 Cnc e
   (~2000 K) extended. At 1015 K equilibrium, the substellar
   hemisphere reaches an airless peak of ~1250 K (derived), enough for
   partial melt in a volatile-bearing basaltic crust but well short of
   a fully molten dayside.

3. **Atmospheric loss.** Any primordial H/He envelope of a 4.7 M⊕ core
   at 0.038 AU around a K dwarf is photoevaporated on a timescale far
   shorter than the system age (Owen & Wu 2017 valley physics); the
   high measured density independently confirms there is no thick
   envelope. A secondary atmosphere is marginal at best at this
   insolation.

**Color choice.** Under the host's 4858 K K3 V illumination (warm
orange SED, peaking near 0.60 μm), surface tints are perceived warmer
than under Sol but with far less of the deep-red M-dwarf shift. The
primary surface tint `#2a2422` is a dark basaltic / partial-melt crust
tone, slightly warmer than the fresh ultramafic black used for the
hottest M-dwarf rocky planets. The accent `#b84a1e` encodes the
substellar cooling-lava glow — a tie-break (interesting-first) that
gives b a visually distinctive feature rather than a uniformly dark
disk; the ~1250 K substellar partial-melt zone radiates with a visible
red-orange tail.

**Morphology under tidal lock.** The substellar partial-melt cap is a
permanent feature, fixed in the surface frame, surrounded by cooled
basaltic plains. The antistellar hemisphere is dark, volatile-stripped
regolith with no cold-trap (no atmosphere to transport volatiles from
the dayside). Sparse impact cratering accumulates over the system age,
partially resurfaced near the substellar point by past melt episodes.

## Atmosphere synthesis

The atmospheric inference for b is theoretical but strongly
constrained by the measured density. There is no JWST transit or
eclipse spectrum yet, so the cfg adopts the **airless** interpretation
with a vestigial silicate-vapor exosphere near the substellar point.

**Pressure choice.** Surface pressure 0 Pa (airless). The decisive
argument is the bulk density: at 6.4 g/cc, b is rock-and-iron with no
room for a thick volatile envelope, and Owen & Wu 2017's
photoevaporation-valley physics places a 4.7 M⊕ core at 0.038 AU
firmly in the core-only regime — any initial H/He envelope is lost on
≲ 10²–10³ Myr around a K dwarf. A secondary outgassed atmosphere would
need continuous replenishment exceeding the XUV-driven loss; at
1015 K equilibrium the balance is marginal, so the cfg picks airless.

**Composition.** No hydrostatic atmosphere. A tenuous silicate-vapor
exosphere is expected over the substellar partial-melt zone
(Schaefer & Fegley 2009 hot-rocky outgassing chemistry: Na, K, SiO
dominant by vapor pressure), but the column density is far below any
Rayleigh-scattering threshold and contributes no visible limb haze.

**Sky appearance.** No atmosphere means no Rayleigh scattering — the
sky is black at every elevation. HD 219134 dominates b's dayside sky
at angular diameter ~10.1° (about 20× the Sun's apparent size from
Earth — by far the largest object in the sky), a warm-orange disk
(4858 K → orange) flooding the surface with ~181× Earth-noon
brightness. The other planets appear as bright points; c (next out)
is the most prominent at conjunction. The limb is a clean edge from
disk to space, with no scattering or refraction.

## Rotation & spin synthesis

The 3.0937-day orbital period at 0.0382 AU around a 0.696 M☉ host
gives a tidal-locking timescale of ~10⁵–10⁶ years (standard
equilibrium-tide estimate, Hut 1981), orders of magnitude shorter than
the system age. The 1:1 spin-orbit resonance is fully established, and
obliquity has damped to zero over the same timescale.

**Spin-orbit state.** The curated eccentricity is 0.0, so there is no
ambiguity between 1:1 and 3:2 — at essentially zero eccentricity the
1:1 lock is overwhelmingly favored (3:2 traps require e ≳ 0.01;
Vinson 2017, Makarov 2018). The substellar point is fixed in the
surface frame.

**KSP implementation note.** Rotation period = orbital period =
3.0937 days (267 296 s). In Kopernicus the body's `rotationPeriod`
should equal the orbital `period` in seconds.

**No seasons.** Obliquity = 0 and eccentricity = 0 give a static
insolation pattern — the substellar partial-melt cap does not migrate
or pulse, unlike the eccentric planets in the system. Tidal heating at
the circular orbit is small (Bolmont 2020 scaling); the surface energy
budget is insolation-dominated, so the partial-melt cap is
insolation-driven rather than tidally driven (unlike Io).

## Visual styling

Combining the surface and atmosphere decisions:

- **Global appearance (orbit view).** A dark-bodied rocky super-Earth
  with a glowing red-orange cap at the substellar point and dark
  near-uniform basaltic plains fading to a black nightside. The
  substellar cooling-lava glow is b's most distinctive visual feature.
- **Substellar partial-melt cap.** A modest disk centred on the
  substellar point, surface tint `#b84a1e` (cooling-lava red-orange),
  with additional thermal-emission brightness at λ ≳ 1 μm in the IR
  cfg. Permanent and fixed (insolation-driven, circular orbit).
- **Mid-latitude basaltic crust.** Tint `#2a2422` (dark basalt /
  partial-melt). Sparse impact craters; smooth cooled lava plains
  predominate.
- **Nightside.** Cold (~150 K) and dark; same tint as mid-latitudes but
  unlit. Visible only by sister-planet reflected light. Volatile-
  stripped regolith.
- **Terminator band.** Sharp, due to the absence of atmospheric
  scattering. Topography casts long shadows in the grazing orange
  light.
- **Atmosphere haze.** None visible; the silicate-vapor exosphere is
  far below any Rayleigh threshold. The limb is a clean edge.
- **Star in sky.** HD 219134 subtends ~10.1° at b — about 20× the Sun's
  angular size from Earth, the dominant object in the sky. Warm-orange
  (4858 K), illuminating the surface at ~181× Earth-noon brightness.
- **Sister planets in sky.** c (next out at 0.064 AU) is the most
  prominent neighbor at inferior conjunction; the warm RV-only planets
  and distant giant h appear as bright points. Near-coplanar geometry
  for the transiting pair.

- **Gillon M. et al. 2017** — *Two massive rocky planets transiting a
  K-dwarf 6.5 parsecs away*, Nat. Astron. 1, 0056
  (`2017NatAs...1E..56G`, doi:10.1038/s41550-017-0056). Spitzer
  photometry establishing b's transit: R = 1.602 ± 0.055 R⊕, true mass
  4.74 ± 0.19 M⊕, T_eq ≈ 1015 K; b and c identified as among the
  densest known transiting super-Earths. **The anchor for b's mass,
  radius, density, and T_eq.**
- **Motalebi F. et al. 2015** — *The HARPS-N Rocky Planet Search. I.
  HD 219134 b: A transiting rocky planet in a multi-planet system*,
  A&A 584, A72 (`2015A&A...584A..72M`). RV discovery of b and the
  3.0937 d / 0.0382 AU orbit; first transit detection. Source for the
  orbital elements.
- **Léger A. et al. 2009** — *Transiting exoplanets from the CoRoT
  space mission VIII. CoRoT-7b: the first super-Earth with measured

The b-specific arXiv record is small. The not-read set is

## Open items for follow-up

- **JWST thermal emission / eclipse.** b is a prime bright-host
  bare-rock target; a MIRI secondary eclipse would directly measure
  the dayside brightness temperature and Bond albedo, confirming or
  refuting the airless reading and refining the surface-tint hex codes
  from inferred mineralogy.
- **Cfg variant: thin retained atmosphere.** A small fraction of
  high-mean-molecular-weight secondary atmospheres (CO₂ / silicate-
  vapor, ≲ 0.1–1 bar) remains formally possible despite the high
  density. A cfg variant with a thin hazy atmosphere is preserved for
  users who prefer the "retained atmosphere" reading.
- **Surface mineralogy.** Hex codes are tie-break choices; future
  thermal-emission spectroscopy (basalt vs ultramafic vs silica-rich)
  would refine them.
- **Tidal heating refinement.** The 0.05–0.5 W/m² range is a Bolmont
  2020 scaling at e ≈ 0; a dedicated interior-structure model would
  tighten it, though insolation dominates regardless.
