# HD 219134 c — Phase 3 Synthesis

HD 219134 c is the second transiting planet of the system, just outside
b, around the bright quiet K3 V dwarf HD 219134. Like b it has both a
measured true mass and a measured radius (Gillon et al. 2017, Spitzer
photometry): M = 4.36 ± 0.22 M⊕, R = 1.511 ± 0.047 R⊕, on a 6.765-day
orbit at a = 0.064 AU with a small eccentricity e = 0.062. The bulk
density is the highest in the system, ρ ≈ 7.0 g/cc — even denser than
b, firmly rocky/iron-rich with no volatile envelope. It is cooler than
b: TEPCat lists T_eq ≈ 782 K, and the project formula
T_eq = 278.3 · L^0.25 / √a (L = 0.264) gives 789 K at zero albedo.

**Scenario choice for NearStars: a hot, very dense, airless rocky
super-Earth with a dark basaltic surface and a faint oxidized /
sub-melt accent — cooler than b, below the basalt melt point, so no
substellar magma cap but still a baked, atmosphere-poor rock.** At
789 K and ~64× Earth's insolation, c is below the partial-melt
threshold but far too hot to retain water; the extreme measured
density (7.0 g/cc) again rules out a thick volatile envelope. The
alternative — a retained thin CO₂ atmosphere — is preserved as a cfg
variant in Open items.

## Surface synthesis

HD 219134 c is the densest planet in the system and the second of the
two transiting rocky super-Earths. Three anchors pin the synthesis:

1. **Measured mass and radius.** Gillon 2017's Spitzer transit gives
   R = 1.511 ± 0.047 R⊕ and the combined transit + RV solution gives
   M = 4.36 ± 0.22 M⊕. The bulk density ρ ≈ 7.0 g/cc (derived) is the
   highest of any planet in the system — even denser than b — and
   firmly rocky/iron-rich, with no room for a volatile envelope.
   Gillon 2017 highlight the b/c pair as among the densest known
   transiting super-Earths.

2. **Insolation 64.5 S⊕, T_eq ≈ 789 K.** Cooler than b but still
   intensely hot. The substellar airless peak reaches ~970 K
   (derived), which is below the basalt solidus (~1300 K) — so unlike
   b, c has no substellar magma cap. It is a baked, dry rock rather
   than a lava world.

3. **Atmospheric loss.** As with b, any primordial H/He envelope was
   photoevaporated long ago (Owen & Wu 2017), and the extreme density
   confirms there is no thick envelope. A secondary atmosphere is
   marginal at 789 K under the close-in XUV flux.

**Color choice.** Under the 4858 K K3 V orange illumination, c's
surface is rendered as a dark baked basaltic crust `#3a3026` — slightly
browner and lighter than b's near-melt `#2a2422`, reflecting the cooler
unmelted state. The accent `#8a3a1a` is a baked iron-oxide / regolith
red-brown rather than a lava glow (a tie-break, interesting-first): c
is below the melt point, so its distinctive feature is oxidized
patchiness, not a glowing substellar cap.

**Morphology under tidal lock.** With no surface melt, c's appearance
is closer to a strongly-insolated Mercury: impact-cratered basaltic
plains accumulated over the system age, baked and volatile-stripped,
with no magma resurfacing to erase craters. The substellar hemisphere
is the hottest but still solid; the antistellar hemisphere is cold,
dark regolith with no cold-trap.

## Atmosphere synthesis

The atmospheric inference for c is theoretical but, like b, strongly
constrained by the measured density. The cfg adopts the **airless**
interpretation.

**Pressure choice.** Surface pressure 0 Pa (airless). At 7.0 g/cc, c
is the densest planet in the system — pure rock and iron with no
volatile envelope, and Owen & Wu 2017's photoevaporation physics
places a 4.4 M⊕ core at 0.064 AU in the core-only regime. A secondary
atmosphere at 789 K is marginal; the cfg picks airless. Because c is
cooler than b, even surface outgassing into an exosphere is weaker, so
c is effectively a bare rock.

**Composition.** No hydrostatic atmosphere. Any surface-sourced
exosphere (Schaefer & Fegley 2009 scaling) is fainter than b's because
the lower substellar temperature suppresses silicate volatilization;
the column density is far below any Rayleigh threshold and gives no
visible limb haze.

**Sky appearance.** No atmosphere means a uniformly black sky.
HD 219134 dominates c's dayside sky at angular diameter ~6.0° (about
12× the Sun's apparent size from Earth), a warm-orange disk flooding
the surface with ~64× Earth-noon brightness. The inner planet b appears
as a bright point at conjunction; the outer planets are fainter. The
limb is a clean edge from disk to space.

## Rotation & spin synthesis

The 6.765-day orbital period at 0.064 AU around a 0.696 M☉ host gives a
tidal-locking timescale far shorter than the system age (Hut 1981), so
the 1:1 spin-orbit resonance is fully established and obliquity has
damped to zero.

**Spin-orbit state.** The curated eccentricity is 0.062 — small but
non-zero. This sits just above the e ≈ 0.01 boundary below which 1:1
is the only stable state, so a 3:2 resonance trap is in principle
possible by analogy with Mercury (e = 0.205). However, slow
eccentricity damping in a compact multi-planet chain favors 1:1 as the
likely endpoint; the cfg adopts 1:1 (substellar point fixed) and notes
the 3:2 alternative as a cfg variant in Open items.

**KSP implementation note.** Rotation period = orbital period =
6.765 days (584 496 s). In Kopernicus the body's `rotationPeriod`
should equal the orbital `period` in seconds.

**No seasons.** Obliquity damped to zero. The small eccentricity
(e = 0.062) gives a peri-to-apo insolation range of
S = (1 ± e)⁻² × 64.5 ≈ 57 → 73 S⊕ over the orbit, but with a fixed
substellar point this manifests as a slight modulation of the dayside
temperature rather than seasonal weather. Tidal heating at e = 0.062
is modest (Bolmont 2020 scaling); the surface energy budget is
insolation-dominated.

## Visual styling

Combining the surface and atmosphere decisions:

- **Global appearance (orbit view).** A dark-bodied rocky super-Earth,
  baked but unmelted, with dark basaltic plains and faint oxidized
  red-brown patchiness, fading to a black nightside. No glowing
  substellar cap (unlike b) — c is below the melt point.
- **Dayside.** Hot (~970 K substellar) baked basalt, tint `#3a3026`,
  with `#8a3a1a` oxidized-iron accent patches. Mercury-analog
  cratered terrain rather than smooth lava plains.
- **Nightside.** Cold (~130 K) and dark; same tint as the dayside but
  unlit. Visible only by sister-planet reflected light.
- **Terminator band.** Sharp, due to the absence of atmospheric
  scattering. Crater rims and ridges throw long shadows in the
  grazing orange light.
- **Atmosphere haze.** None visible; airless. The limb is a clean edge.
- **Star in sky.** HD 219134 subtends ~6.0° at c — about 12× the Sun's
  angular size from Earth. Warm-orange (4858 K), illuminating the
  surface at ~64× Earth-noon brightness.
- **Sister planets in sky.** b (inner, at 0.0382 AU) is the most
  prominent neighbor at inferior conjunction, near-coplanar; the warm
  RV-only planets and distant giant h appear as bright points.

## Open items for follow-up

- **JWST thermal emission / eclipse.** Like b, c is a bright-host
  bare-rock target; a MIRI eclipse would measure the dayside
  brightness temperature and Bond albedo and refine the surface tints.
- **Cfg variant: thin retained CO₂ atmosphere.** A thin
  high-mean-molecular-weight atmosphere (≲ 0.1–1 bar CO₂) remains
  formally possible. A cfg variant with a thin hazy atmosphere is
  preserved for the "retained atmosphere" reading.
- **Cfg variant: 3:2 spin-orbit resonance.** At e = 0.062, c sits just
  above the 1:1-only boundary; a Mercury-analog 3:2 variant would
  render with a slowly drifting hottest-longitude. Document as a
  Phase 3.5 alternative.
- **Surface mineralogy.** Hex codes are tie-break choices; future
  thermal-emission spectroscopy would refine them.
